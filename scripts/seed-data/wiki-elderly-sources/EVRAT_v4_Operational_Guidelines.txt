1. Introduction
The Elder Vulnerability Rapid Assessment Tool (EVRAT) is a structured community caregiving assessment framework for elders living in vulnerable urban settlements and resettlement communities.
The framework supports:
	•	vulnerability categorisation
	•	caregiving prioritisation
	•	dynamic reassessment
	•	follow-up planning
	•	crisis identification
	•	referral planning
	•	prevention of severe unmet needs and suffering
2. Assessment Philosophy
The framework recognises that all elders experience varying degrees of vulnerability, dependency, social risk, health burden, caregiving gaps, and emotional needs.
The purpose of assessment is not to prove vulnerability. It is to understand intensity, prioritise engagement, and ensure that severe needs are not missed.
The framework follows the principles of:
	•	dignity and contextual sensitivity
	•	reduction of unnecessary questioning
	•	progressive layered assessment
	•	dynamic reassessment
	•	prevention-oriented caregiving
	•	reduction of subjective variation through operational definitions
	•	informed consent and data dignity  (added in v4)
Vishnu Harikumar: Recommendation 12 — Adding consent and data dignity as an explicit principle. The full operational rules are in the new Section 20.
3. Overall Assessment Architecture
The assessment system follows a layered structure:

+ NEW SECTION  •  3.1 Contextual modifier — Living Arrangement (added in v4)
Living arrangement is captured as a baseline contextual field (in Section A of the Instrument), not a scored item. It modifies how other findings are interpreted: a high dependency score in an elder living alone requires a different response from the same score in a household with adult children. Structured options include: lives alone, with spouse only, with children/family, with grandchildren only (skip-generation), with relatives, with non-kin/neighbour, institutional, homeless/unstable housing.
Vishnu Harikumar: Recommendation 11 — Living arrangement was free text in v3 (Section B only, Stable elders only). Vulnerable elders going through EVA had no living arrangement field at all. Now captured for everyone as a structured baseline field.
4. Initial Vulnerability Screening (IVS)
The IVS functions as a first-level relevance screening mechanism. It is intended to:
	•	identify elders who appear currently stable and self-managing
	•	identify elders requiring deeper assessment
	•	reduce respondent fatigue
	•	reduce unnecessary assessment burden
	•	improve field efficiency
	•	prioritise assessment of vulnerable elders
The IVS does not generate final vulnerability categories. It only determines whether expanded vulnerability assessment should proceed.
+ NEW SECTION  •  4.1 Field markers — Baseline vs. Visit (added in v4)
Every field in the EVRAT Instrument is marked with one of three indicators:
	•	[B] — Baseline: captured once at first assessment; updated only when something changes (address, household composition, entitlements gained, tier reassigned).
	•	[V] — Visit: captured fresh at every assessment, including reassessments. Trajectory is read from these.
	•	[B/V] — Captured at baseline; refreshed at visit if changed.
Reassessment generates a new record — never overwrite previous data. Trajectory is read by comparing visits over time. The previous reassessment's Section E2 (Care Plan Tasks) is opened first; each open task reviewed and status-updated; only then does the CO proceed to a fresh EVA.
Vishnu Harikumar: Recommendation 3 — This converts EVRAT from a snapshot into a longitudinal instrument without adding any fields. Tells the CO and the digital app which fields to lock vs. re-open at each reassessment.

7. Stable & Self-Managing Category
An elder may be temporarily categorised as Stable & Self-Managing only when the elder:
	•	appears functionally independent or largely independent
	•	remains socially, physically, emotionally, or economically engaged
	•	has no major visible concern
	•	has not experienced major destabilising events recently
	•	has reasonably dependable support available
Stable status indicates that immediate intensive support engagement is not currently required. However, all elders in this category remain under periodic review (3-monthly minimum).

+ NEW SECTION  •  Note on checkbox-derived items (added in v4)
Q8a (ADL) and Q8b (IADL) are checkbox-driven. The CO ticks each activity for which help is needed. The 0–3 score is derived from the count:
	•	0 activities ticked → score 0 (independent)
	•	1 activity → score 1
	•	2–3 activities → score 2
	•	4+ activities → score 3
The digital application performs this derivation automatically. On paper, the CO counts and assigns.
Final category generation combines:
	•	weighted total score (against cut-offs in Section 11)
	•	critical risk flags (override to Profound regardless of score)
	•	supervisor validation where applicable

12. Detailed Assessment Trigger Logic
Detailed Assessment should be prioritised for:
	•	Moderate Need category
	•	High Need category
	•	Profound Need category
	•	any elder with Critical Risk Flags
	•	any elder referred by supervisors
	•	any elder referred through field observations or staff concern
13. Dynamic Reassessment Philosophy
The assessment framework is dynamic and editable. Categories may change over time based on:
	•	health deterioration
	•	falls or accidents
	•	hospitalisation
	•	caregiving collapse
	•	emotional deterioration
	•	bereavement
	•	violence or abuse
	•	environmental change
	•	food insecurity
	•	field observations
14. Event Trigger Framework
Specific events trigger immediate reassessment irrespective of cadence:
	•	Fall or fracture
	•	Hospitalisation
	•	Death of caregiver
	•	Widowhood
	•	Violence or abuse
	•	Visible health deterioration
	•	Severe emotional distress
	•	New dependency
	•	Food insecurity
	•	Unsafe shelter
	•	Major illness diagnosis
	•	Displacement or eviction
15. Reassessment Authority and Workflow
Any authorised staff member with access to elder records may:
	•	trigger reassessment
	•	raise concern alerts
	•	recommend escalation
	•	request supervisor review
Stable & Self-Managing status should not remain unreviewed beyond 3 months.
16. Supervisor Validation Logic
Downgrade from severe categories (High or Profound Need) should require:
	•	reassessment
	•	documentation
	•	supervisor validation
This prevents underestimation of severe vulnerability and protects against premature de-escalation.

+ NEW SECTION  •  20. Consent and Sensitive Field Handling (added in v4)
20.1 Consent
Verbal consent is captured at baseline (Section A of the Instrument). The CO reads the consent statement aloud in the elder's preferred language. Annual re-confirmation is required.
The consent statement:
"This assessment is being conducted to understand your situation and support you better. The information you share will be kept confidential and used only by the programme team to help you access care, services, and entitlements. You can choose not to answer any question, and you can ask us to remove your information at any time. Do you agree to participate?"
For elders with cognitive impairment, seek consent from the elder where possible and from the primary caregiver. If an elder later asks to be removed from the programme, the request is honoured and documented.
20.2 Sensitive fields
The following fields are marked [Sensitive] in the Instrument:
	•	Q6b — Caregiver burden (the caregiver's voice; must not be shown to elder or other family)
	•	Q15a — Depression screen (mental health stigma)
	•	Q15b — Loneliness (privacy of personal state)
	•	Q16 — Abuse or neglect (could put elder at greater risk if disclosed)
	•	Any Section E observations naming violence, financial exploitation, or family conflict
20.3 Restricted access
Sensitive fields are visible to the CO conducting the assessment and to the programme manager / city lead. They are not visible on:
	•	field-level printouts carried by COs
	•	peer dashboards or shared MIS views accessible to non-elderly-team staff
	•	external reports (anonymised aggregate indicators only)
Anonymised summary indicators (e.g., 'abuse concern flagged: yes/no') may appear in aggregate reports.
20.4 Physical security
If the instrument is paper-based, sensitive-marked pages are kept in the locked office file; not discussed with neighbours, other family members, or other COs except the supervisor. If digital, sensitive fields are encrypted and access-controlled per the MIS specification.
Vishnu Harikumar: Recommendation 12 — v3 collected sensitive information (abuse, mental health, financial status) with no consent statement, no sensitive-field marking, and no access protocol. This was a real programme risk — for elders, for caregivers, and for the organisation. The 30-second cost of asking for consent is far outweighed by the safeguarding gain, and the restricted-view rule protects elders from harm by inadvertent disclosure.

Section E of the EVRAT Instrument has been restructured into five sub-sections (E1–E5) to convert the assessment outcome into a structured, trackable care plan.

Purpose

	•	Caregiver support (respite, training, peer connection)
	•	Safety (GBV referral, housing safety, property)
	•	Other

	•	Not started
	•	In progress — documents being gathered
	•	In progress — submitted, awaiting response
	•	Stuck — missing document
	•	Stuck — portal / system issue
	•	Stuck — elder unable / unwilling
	•	Stuck — family / household resistance
	•	Stuck — awaiting referral from PHC / govt

	•	Done — linkage confirmed

21.3 Reassessment rhythm
At every reassessment, the CO opens the previous assessment's Section E2 first. Each open task is reviewed: what moved, what didn't, why is it stuck? Only after this review does the CO proceed to a fresh EVA. Reassessment is a continuation, not a restart.
Tasks closed at any visit remain on record. New tasks are appended. Trajectory across an elder's relationship with the programme is preserved through this append-only structure.

+ NEW SECTION  •  22. Calibration and Inter-rater Reliability (added in v4)
Because EVRAT relies on assessor judgement for many items, two assessors observing the same elder may produce different scores. The following calibration protocol reduces variation before rollout and detects drift during operation.
22.1 Pre-rollout calibration
Conducted once, before any CO uses EVRAT in the field.
	•	Identify 5 elders across the vulnerability spectrum (1 likely Stable, 2 likely Moderate, 1 likely High, 1 likely Profound).
	•	Three COs and one supervisor assess each elder independently, on the same day, without consulting each other.
	•	Scores compared item by item. Items where COs disagree by ≥2 points are flagged for discussion.
	•	The team reviews each disagreement: was the operational definition unclear? Did one CO see something others missed? Refine the operational definition in the guidelines if needed, or refine training if the definition is fine but applied inconsistently.
	•	Repeat with a fresh set of 5 elders until agreement on ≥80% of items is achieved.
22.2 Quarterly calibration
Conducted every quarter during operation.
	•	Each quarter, two COs jointly visit 2 elders together — one in the calmer middle of the day, after the regular assessment is done.
	•	Each CO completes the EVRAT independently from observation.
	•	Scores compared at the next team meeting. Disagreements ≥2 points discussed and definitions refined.
	•	Results documented; major drift triggers a refresher training.
22.3 What calibration is — and what it is not
Calibration is a tool-improvement exercise, not a performance evaluation of COs. Disagreement is expected and useful — it reveals where the tool is weak, not where the CO is wrong. The calibration record focuses on the tool, not the individual.
Without calibration, the same elder may be scored differently by different COs over time, and trajectory comparisons become noise rather than signal. Calibration is the only mechanism that prevents this drift.
Vishnu Harikumar: Recommendation 15 — Calibration converts EVRAT from a form-with-definitions into an instrument with measured reliability. The 80% agreement threshold is reasonable for a 22-item tool with 0–3 scaling — if it proves easy to clear, raise the bar in v5.
