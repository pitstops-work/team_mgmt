How to use this manual
After an assessment, the care plan tells you WHAT an elder needs. This manual tells you HOW to do each of those things. One short chapter per activity. Find the activity, read the chapter, do the work.
Every chapter has the same simple shape: what the activity is, when you do it, the steps to do it, what to watch for in a slum setting, and how you know you are finished. Some chapters also have a DIGNITY note (how to do it without taking away the elder's pride) and a DO NOT line (the mistakes that cause harm).
Three things that are true of every activity in this manual
	•	You are looking across at an equal, never down at a poor old person. The elder decides about their own life. You help; you do not take over.
	•	Doing it once is not doing it. You follow through until the thing is actually real — the pension in the account, the elder at the camp, the aid in the hand. “I tried” is not “I did.”
	•	Ask the elder what THEY want before you decide what they need. Often it is different from what you assumed.
Owner codes used here: CO = you, the Community Organiser. KI = Kitchen In-charge. CI = Centre In-charge. Sup = Supervisor. ET = Entitlements Team (a separate team — you refer to them, they process). City Lead = the senior lead for serious cases.

A.  Caregiving & Family
1
Holding a Family Meeting & Caregiving Agreement
Getting the family together to agree, out loud, on who looks after the elder and how — so care does not fall on one exhausted person or fall through the cracks entirely.
WHEN YOU DO IT  When caregiving is patchy or unclear (Q6 = 2), or when one family member is carrying everything and others are absent.
How to do it
	•	Find out first, quietly, who actually does the caring now — not who is supposed to, but who really does. It is often one woman: a wife, a daughter-in-law.
	•	Ask the elder who they trust and who they want help from. Their answer comes first.
	•	Pick a time the working family members are home — usually evening. Tell them it is a short, friendly conversation about amma/appa, not a complaint session.
	•	Sit with the whole family together. Name the care tasks plainly: meals, medicines, bathing, toilet, company, money. Ask: who can take which?
	•	Push gently for backup. If only one person is named for everything, that person will break. Ask who is the second pair of hands.
	•	Write the agreement down simply — who does what, on what days — and read it back so everyone hears it. Leave a copy in the house.
	•	Set a date to check back. An agreement nobody revisits dies in a week.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  In a 180-square-foot home with everyone working daily wage, there may be no slack at all — the family is not unwilling, they are stretched to breaking. Do not moralise. Look for what is actually possible, and fill the rest with kitchen, centre, and your own visits.
DIGNITY  Talk about the elder, with the elder present and included — never over their head as if they are furniture. Ask their view at every step. They ran a household once; they are not a task on a list.
DO NOT  Do not shame the family or take sides in front of everyone. If you turn the meeting into blame, the door closes and the elder loses their advocate inside the house.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  The family has named who does what, there is a written copy in the home, a backup person exists, and you have a date to check it is holding.

2
Training a Caregiver
Teaching the family member (or paid helper) who looks after the elder the practical skills to do it safely and without hurting themselves or the elder.
WHEN YOU DO IT  Whenever an elder needs hands-on help — bathing, lifting, medicines, a bedridden elder. Especially when a fall, illness, or new dependency has just changed things.
How to do it
	•	Watch how the caregiver does it now, before teaching. They have been managing; respect that, and build on it.
	•	Teach the few things that matter most, by showing, not lecturing: how to help an elder up without hurting either back; how to turn a bedridden elder; how to keep skin dry; how to give medicines on time.
	•	Have the caregiver do it while you watch, and correct gently. People learn with their hands, not their ears.
	•	Write or draw the medicine routine and stick it on the wall where it is seen daily.
	•	Tell them plainly what danger signs mean ‘call me or the PHC today’ — fever, a wound, not passing urine, sudden confusion, a fall.
	•	Come back within a week to check the skills stuck, and to encourage. Praise what they are doing right.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  The caregiver is often herself tired, unwell, or also caring for children. Teaching her is also caring for her — ask how she is managing. A caregiver who feels seen will keep going; one who feels used will not.
DIGNITY  Teach in a way that lets the elder keep doing what they still can. Never train the caregiver to do FOR the elder what the elder can still do themselves — that steals independence and speeds decline.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  The caregiver can do the key tasks safely while you watch, knows the danger signs and whom to call, and the medicine routine is on the wall.

3
Arranging Caregiver Respite & Support
Giving the main caregiver a break and a circle of support, so they do not burn out and start — without meaning to — neglecting the elder.
WHEN YOU DO IT  When the caregiver shows strain (Q6b = 2) or burnout (Q6b = 3): exhausted, short-tempered, sleepless, unwell, or saying they cannot manage.
How to do it
	•	Talk to the caregiver alone, kindly. Ask how THEY are — not how the elder is. Let them say the hard thing.
	•	Find a few hours of relief: another family member to take a shift, a neighbour to sit, or the elder attending the centre for a day.
	•	Connect them to other caregivers — the caregiver support group at the centre. Knowing others carry the same weight lightens it.
	•	If burnout is severe, treat it as urgent: arrange the respite within two weeks, bring in the supervisor, and look at sharing the load across the family.
	•	Check the medicines and the elder's basic care are still happening while the caregiver is stretched — burnout is where quiet neglect begins.
	•	Keep checking on the caregiver, not just the elder, at every visit.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  The daughter-in-law caring for a bedridden elder while raising children is at the highest risk and the least likely to complain — complaining can cause family trouble for her. Ask her directly and privately. An aged spouse caring for another aged spouse is just as fragile.
DIGNITY  Respite is not a luxury or a weakness. Say so plainly to a guilty caregiver: ‘Resting so you can keep caring is part of caring.’
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  The caregiver has real, regular relief; is connected to other caregivers; and the elder's basic care is confirmed to be holding.

4
When There Is No Caregiver
Building a patchwork of support around an elder who lives alone or has no one reliable — so they are not left to manage a failing body by themselves.
WHEN YOU DO IT  When the elder lives alone with no dependable support (Q21 = 3), or the critical flag ‘no dependable caregiver’ is raised.
How to do it
	•	Map who is already nearby: a neighbour who looks in, a shopkeeper who knows them, a distant relative, the settlement leader. Even loose connections matter.
	•	Ask the elder who they would call in trouble — and whether that person actually answers.
	•	Build a daily touch-point: the community kitchen meal delivery doubles as a daily check that the elder is alright; centre attendance if they can walk there.
	•	Identify one willing neighbour as the ‘first call’ and make sure they have your number and the elder agrees.
	•	Where there is genuinely no one and the elder is dependent, raise with the supervisor whether a paid community caregiver (a trusted local woman, small honorarium) can be arranged.
	•	Make your own visits more frequent and reliable — for this elder, you are part of the safety net.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  An elder living alone is not always lonely or in danger — some are at peace and capable. Do not impose help that wounds their pride. But a dependent elder living alone with no one is a quiet emergency. Tell the two apart by watching, and by asking them.
DIGNITY  Living alone can be a choice and a strength, not only a problem. Honour the elder who manages alone and wants to. Offer the net without insisting they are helpless.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  The elder has at least one reliable contact within a few minutes’ reach, a daily touch-point exists, and you and a named neighbour both watch over them.

B.  Mobility & the Body
5
Checking the Home for Hazards & Fixing Them
Walking through the elder's home with their eyes — finding the things that will make them fall or get hurt, and fixing the cheap, simple ones.
WHEN YOU DO IT  When an elder is unsteady, has fallen, is mostly confined (Q7 = 2), or whenever you see a clear hazard. Do it early for any frail elder.
How to do it
	•	Walk the path the elder actually takes every day: bed to toilet, bed to door, sitting place to kitchen. Crouch to their height and pace.
	•	Look for the common killers: no light at night, a high threshold to trip on, a loose mat, a wet slippery toilet area, clutter on the floor path, nothing to hold while standing up.
	•	Fix the easy ones on the spot or within days: a night light or torch by the bed, remove the loose mat, clear the path, add a sturdy thing to hold near the bed and toilet.
	•	For bigger fixes — a handrail, a raised toilet seat — arrange them through the programme; talk to the settlement leader for any structural issue.
	•	Check footwear. Worn-out slippers cause falls. A good pair of non-slip slippers is one of the cheapest, best things you can give.
	•	Come back and confirm the changes are still in place — mats reappear, lights stop working.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  In a one-room home you cannot rearrange much, and the floor may flood in monsoon. Work with what is real. Even one night light and a cleared path to the toilet prevents the night fall that breaks a hip.
DIGNITY  Ask before you move or remove anything — it is their home, not your project. Explain why. An elder who agrees to the change keeps it; one who feels overruled puts the mat back.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  The daily path is clear and lit, there is something safe to hold near bed and toilet, footwear is safe, and the elder agreed to every change.

6
Getting the Right Mobility Aid
Putting the right walking aid — stick, walker, wheelchair, or commode — into the elder's hands, fitted to them and actually used.
WHEN YOU DO IT  When the elder needs support to walk (Q9 = 2), cannot walk (Q9 = 3), or after falls. A commode when reaching the toilet is hard or dangerous.
How to do it
	•	Match the aid to the need, not to what is in stock: a stick for mild unsteadiness; a walker for more; a wheelchair if they cannot walk; a bedside commode if the toilet is far or unsafe.
	•	Get the size and height right. A stick or walker at the wrong height does more harm than good — elbow slightly bent, wrist at hip height when standing.
	•	Show them how to use it properly and watch them try. Many elders use a stick wrongly and it makes them less safe.
	•	Procure it through the programme stock or partner. Open the disability-certificate route with the Entitlements Team if they qualify — it unlocks free aids and schemes.
	•	Make sure it is actually used, not stood in the corner out of pride or habit. Ask why if it is not, and solve that reason.
	•	Check it stays in good repair — worn rubber tips on a stick make it slip.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  Pride stops many elders using an aid — ‘people will think I am finished.’ Acknowledge that feeling rather than dismissing it. Sometimes a walking stick that looks dignified is used where a walker is refused.
DIGNITY  An aid is a tool for independence, not a badge of decline — frame it that way. ‘This lets you go out again,’ not ‘this is because you are weak.’
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  The elder has the right aid, fitted correctly, knows how to use it, and is actually using it day to day.

7
Preventing Falls
Stopping the next fall — because for an old person one fall often begins the slide into being bedridden, and the second fall is more likely than the first.
WHEN YOU DO IT  After any fall (Q10a = 1, 2, or 3), or when an elder is unsteady or afraid of falling (Q10b).
How to do it
	•	Take a fall seriously even if there was no injury. One fall roughly doubles the chance of the next. Find out how and where it happened.
	•	Do the home hazard check (chapter 5) — most falls happen at home, at night, on the way to the toilet.
	•	Get the right walking aid (chapter 6) and safe footwear.
	•	Ask the PHC to review medicines — some BP and sleeping medicines make elders dizzy when they stand up and cause falls. This is often missed.
	•	For repeated falls, push for a medical check of the cause — heart, blood pressure, weakness, vision — through the PHC. Repeated falls are a signal, not bad luck.
	•	If the elder is now afraid to move, do not let fear freeze them into a chair — that weakens them and causes more falls. Build confidence with short, supported walks; a walking buddy; a reason to go out, like the centre.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  Fear of falling is its own problem. The elder who stops moving gets weaker and falls more. Gentle, steady, supported movement is the medicine — not rest.
DIGNITY  Do not scold an elder for falling or wrap them in cotton wool. Help them keep moving safely — a life lived sitting in one chair from fear is its own kind of harm.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  The home is safer, the right aid and footwear are in use, medicines have been reviewed for dizziness, and the elder is moving with confidence rather than frozen by fear.

8
Caring for a Bedridden Elder & Preventing Bed Sores
Keeping a bedridden elder comfortable, clean, and free of bed sores — the painful, dangerous wounds that form fast when someone cannot shift their own weight.
WHEN YOU DO IT  When an elder is mostly confined or completely bedridden (Q7 = 2 or 3). This is high-priority, hands-on work — act within a week.
How to do it
	•	Turn the elder regularly — from side to back to other side every two to three hours, day and night. This is the single most important thing to prevent bed sores. Teach the caregiver this above all.
	•	Check the skin daily at the pressure points — the lower back, hips, heels, shoulders, back of head. Redness that does not fade is the first warning. Catch it here.
	•	Keep skin clean and dry. Wetness from sweat or urine breaks skin down fast. Change and clean promptly; let skin air.
	•	Use a soft surface — an air mattress or even folded soft cloth and pillows to take pressure off the bony points. Arrange an air mattress through the programme if there is bed-sore risk.
	•	Link the PHC for a home visit — the ANM or nurse should see a bedridden elder. If a sore has already formed, it needs proper dressing, not home remedies.
	•	Keep them part of life — move the bed where they can see people, be talked to, see daylight. A bedridden elder facing a blank wall dies in spirit before the body.
	•	Watch for the dangers: a sore that is deepening, fever, the elder not eating or drinking, sudden confusion — these need the PHC the same day.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  Families often do not know that turning prevents sores, and discover the wound only when it is bad and smells. Teach turning early and check the skin yourself — do not just ask, look, with the elder’s permission.
DIGNITY  Bathing, turning, cleaning an elder who cannot do it themselves is where dignity is most easily lost. Cover them, be gentle, explain what you are doing, never expose or rush them, never speak of them as a burden in their hearing.
DO NOT  Do not let a bedridden elder lie unturned for hours, lie in wetness, or be tucked away facing a wall. Each of these causes real, fast harm.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  The caregiver turns the elder every 2–3 hours and checks skin daily, the skin is clean and dry on a soft surface, the PHC is linked, and the elder is part of the household’s life, not hidden away.

9
Continence & Diaper Support
Helping an elder who cannot always control urine or stool stay clean, dry, and — above all — dignified.
WHEN YOU DO IT  When an elder has continence difficulty, often alongside being bedridden or having dementia. Diapers may be part of the care plan.
How to do it
	•	First ask whether it can be improved, not just managed. Reaching the toilet in time may be a mobility or distance problem — a bedside commode (chapter 6) can restore control and pride.
	•	Rule out a quick medical cause with the PHC — a urine infection causes sudden leaking and confusion and is easily treated. Do not assume it is ‘just old age.’
	•	If diapers are needed, arrange a regular supply through the programme — do not let the family run out, which leads to the elder lying in wetness.
	•	Teach the caregiver to change promptly and keep skin clean and dry — wetness causes rashes and bed sores quickly.
	•	Set up a routine — regular toilet or commode times can reduce accidents and the number of diapers needed.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  Incontinence is one of the most shaming things for an elder, and one of the heaviest burdens for a caregiver (constant washing). Both need your matter-of-fact kindness. Running out of diapers is a common, preventable crisis — stay ahead of the supply.
DIGNITY  Never speak of this in front of others, never show distaste, never scold an elder for an accident. Handle it as ordinary care. Your calm, unembarrassed manner lets the elder keep their dignity.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  A medical cause has been ruled out, the elder uses a commode or has a reliable diaper supply, skin is kept clean and dry, and the matter is handled with complete discretion.

C.  Health
10
Linking an Elder to the PHC / UPHC
Connecting the elder to the government primary health centre so their ongoing conditions — BP, sugar, heart, joints — are actually being watched and treated, not just named.
WHEN YOU DO IT  When an elder has chronic illness or several conditions (Q11 = 2), or any health need that the public system should be managing.
How to do it
	•	Find out where the nearest PHC or urban PHC is, its timings, and which days the doctor sits. Know this for your settlement before you need it.
	•	Make one consolidated list of the elder’s conditions and all medicines, with the family. Confusion about medicines is dangerous and common.
	•	Take the elder for a first visit — accompany them if they are alone or unsure. The first visit sets whether they will keep going.
	•	Set up the routine: when is the next check, how often, who takes them. Write it down.
	•	For a bedridden or frail elder, ask the PHC for a home visit by the ANM or nurse — this is their job and they will often come if asked properly.
	•	Keep a simple record of visits and what the doctor said, so care continues even if a different CO visits.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  Elders drop out of PHC care because it is far, the queue is long, the staff are brusque, or no one reminds them. Your job is to smooth all of that — know the staff, pick the quiet day, arrange the company.
DIGNITY  The PHC is the elder’s right as a citizen, not a charity. Carry yourself — and help them carry themselves — as someone claiming what is theirs, not begging a favour.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  The elder is enrolled at the PHC, has a clear check-up routine that is being kept, a consolidated medicine list exists, and a bedridden elder has a home-visit arrangement.

11
Helping an Elder Take Medicines Regularly
Closing the gap between medicines prescribed and medicines actually taken — the gap where most chronic-illness harm happens.
WHEN YOU DO IT  When medicines are taken irregularly (Q12 = 2) or not at all despite being needed (Q12 = 3).
How to do it
	•	First find out WHY they are not taking them — the reason decides the fix. The common reasons: cost, the PHC ran out of stock, they dislike how it makes them feel, they forget, or no one reminds them.
	•	If cost: link to the PHC for free supply, or to a Janaushadhi store for cheap generics.
	•	If PHC stockout: escalate to the PHC officer; this is a system failure, not the elder’s fault.
	•	If they dislike a side-effect: do not dismiss it — take them back to the doctor to adjust. A medicine that makes them feel worse will not be taken.
	•	If forgetting: set up a simple system — a pill box with day compartments, linking doses to daily habits (with morning tea, after dinner), a family member who reminds.
	•	If no one reminds and they live alone: build the reminder into your visits and the kitchen delivery, and into a neighbour’s daily look-in.
	•	Check back that the fix worked — do not assume. Look at the pill box, count what is left.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  ‘Are you taking your medicines?’ almost always gets ‘yes.’ Instead, look — count the pills left against the days passed. The truth is in the strip, not the answer.
DIGNITY  Do not treat an elder like a forgetful child about medicines. Find the real obstacle and remove it. Most ‘non-compliance’ is a cost or supply or side-effect problem, not stubbornness.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  You know why doses were missed, you have fixed that specific reason, and a check of the actual pills shows the elder is now taking them regularly.

12
Making a Hospital / Specialist Referral Work
Making sure a referral to a hospital or specialist actually happens — not just that a piece of paper was handed over.
WHEN YOU DO IT  When the elder needs specialist care or a referral is required (Q13b = 2), or any condition the PHC sends onward.
How to do it
	•	Get clear on exactly where they must go, for what, and by when. A vague referral goes nowhere.
	•	Sort out the practical barriers before the date: how they will travel, who goes with them, whether they can manage the distance, what it costs.
	•	Accompany the elder if they are alone, frail, or unsure — a confused, frightened elder sent alone to a big hospital simply turns back. Transport cost is reimbursable.
	•	Carry the medicine list and any records. Hospitals ask; an elder may not know.
	•	After the visit, find out what happened and what was advised — follow up at home within a few days. Get the discharge slip or prescription, photograph it.
	•	Make sure the next step the hospital advised actually gets done — the referral is not finished until its instructions are carried out.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  The referral that fails is almost always the one where no one solved transport and company. An elder will not, cannot, navigate a government hospital alone. That accompaniment is the whole job here.
DIGNITY  Stay beside the elder in an intimidating hospital — speak for them only when they want you to, never about them as if absent. Let them keep their voice even when frightened.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  The elder actually attended, you know what was advised, the advice is being acted on, and a home follow-up has happened.

13
Eye & Ear Camps, Cataract & Hearing Aids
Getting an elder’s failing eyes and ears checked and fixed — because cataract blindness and deafness are common, reversible, and quietly destroy an elder’s independence and connection.
WHEN YOU DO IT  When the elder has vision or hearing trouble (Q13a = 2 or 3). High priority when severe — the fix changes their whole life.
How to do it
	•	Notice the signs: bumping into things, not recognising faces, cloudy eyes (cataract); asking for repetition, speaking too loud, withdrawing from talk (hearing).
	•	Refer to the next eye or ENT camp, or ask the supervisor to arrange one with a partner hospital for a cluster of elders.
	•	For cataract: this is day surgery, often free at government or partner eye hospitals, and it gives sight back. Make the surgery actually happen — arrange the date, the travel, the company, the follow-up.
	•	For hearing: a basic hearing aid is cheap or free through schemes. Get the elder fitted and shown how to use and maintain it.
	•	Open the disability-certificate route with the Entitlements Team where the loss qualifies — it unlocks aids and benefits.
	•	Follow through to the actual outcome — the elder seeing, the elder hearing — not just the camp attended.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  An elder ‘gone simple’ or ‘gone quiet’ in old age is very often just not seeing or hearing — and is being written off for something fixable. Always check the eyes and ears before assuming the mind.
DIGNITY  Restoring sight or hearing restores a person to their family and their own life. Treat this as among the most powerful things you can do — because it is.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  The elder has been seen, the cataract operated or the hearing aid fitted, and they can actually see or hear better — confirmed, not assumed.

14
A Medical Emergency / Sudden Deterioration
Acting fast and right when an elder is suddenly very unwell — the moments where speed saves a life.
WHEN YOU DO IT  Urgent attention needed (Q13b = 3), or any sudden change: collapse, chest pain, breathing trouble, sudden confusion, a bad fall, stroke signs (face drooping, arm weakness, slurred speech), not waking properly.
How to do it
	•	Decide fast: is this life-threatening? If yes — call 108 for the ambulance immediately. Do not wait, do not arrange a camp, do not call a meeting.
	•	Stay with the elder. Keep them calm and comfortable. Do not leave them alone.
	•	Call the family at once. Call your supervisor as you go.
	•	If not immediately life-threatening but serious, contact the PHC the same day, arrange transport, and get them seen — do not let it wait for the next scheduled visit.
	•	Go with them if the family cannot, or follow to the hospital. A frightened elder should not face an emergency alone.
	•	Afterwards: write down what happened the same day, get the discharge details, and within two days visit home to check medicines and recovery. A hospital stay often changes everything — reassess.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  Stroke and heart attack are time-critical — every minute counts. Learn the stroke signs (face, arm, speech) and the heart signs (chest pain, sweating, breathlessness) by heart. When unsure, treat it as an emergency.
DO NOT  Do not try home remedies, do not wait ‘to see if it passes,’ and do not delay the ambulance to first reach a family member. Call 108 first, then call the family.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  The elder got emergency care fast, the family and supervisor were informed, you stayed with them or followed, and you have done the after-care visit and reassessment.

D.  Mind & Heart
15
Mental Health Support
Helping an elder who is low, hopeless, or not themselves — getting them seen by the clinical psychologist at the weekly camp and making sure the plan that follows actually happens.
WHEN YOU DO IT  When the assessment raises a mental health concern. Severe (low nearly every day, or any talk of wanting to die) — tell the supervisor the SAME DAY, do not wait for the camp. Moderate — route to the next weekly camp.
How to do it
	•	Flag it to the supervisor and note it sensitively — this is private; it stays in the restricted file, not shared with neighbours or other family.
	•	Mark the elder for the weekly camp at the centre. The clinician is Kannada-speaking; if the elder speaks another language, the supervisor arranges a clinician who shares it — no elder is sent to someone they cannot speak to.
	•	Bring the elder to the camp — this is the hard part. Do not call it a ‘mental camp.’ Say there is someone good to talk to, people feel lighter after, you will come along.
	•	Be there when the psychologist gives the plan (with the elder’s consent), so you know what you are now responsible for: either a psychiatrist referral, or counselling sessions plus an activity plan.
	•	If psychiatrist referral: make the appointment actually happen, ensure medicines are taken, visit home a few days after to check.
	•	If counselling + activity: make sure they attend each session and the activity plan runs between sessions — through the caregiver if there is one; through the centre if the elder is alone (which also treats the loneliness).
	•	Keep watching. Mental health does not ‘close.’ Set a check-in rhythm and reopen at once if they slip.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  Distress hides — as body aches, as anger, as ‘I am a burden,’ as simply stopping. Learn to see it behind these masks. And the family’s shame is often the biggest barrier to the camp — work on that gently before forcing anything.
DIGNITY  Never name it as madness. Sit with the elder, not above them. Ask privately, never in front of family who may be part of the pain.
DO NOT  If there is ANY talk of wanting to die, any plan to self-harm — this stops being this chapter and becomes the Safety Pathway. Tell the supervisor the same day. Do not wait for the camp. Do not carry it alone.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  The elder has been seen by the psychologist, the plan (psychiatrist or counselling+activity) is actually running, the elder is participating, the distress is not worsening, and a check-in rhythm is set.

16
Dementia & Confusion — Keeping the Elder Safe
Protecting an elder whose memory and thinking are failing — keeping them safe from the dangers confusion brings, and supporting the family who care for them.
WHEN YOU DO IT  When the elder has memory loss or confusion (Q14 = 2), or severe confusion or unsafe wandering (Q14 = 3).
How to do it
	•	First, get a medical check — some confusion is reversible. A urine infection, a medicine reaction, low B12, or thyroid trouble can cause sudden confusion that clears when treated. Do not assume it is permanent dementia. Route to the PHC.
	•	Make the home safe for someone who forgets: control access to the stove and matches, lock away dangerous things and medicines, secure money, prevent wandering out and getting lost.
	•	Put identification on the elder — a band or a card with their name, the family’s phone number, and address — so a wandering elder is brought home.
	•	Set up a simple, steady routine — same times, same faces, calm surroundings. Confusion worsens with chaos and change.
	•	Support the family heavily — caring for a confused elder is exhausting and frightening. Teach them not to argue with or shame the elder for forgetting; it only distresses everyone.
	•	Keep the elder engaged and calm — familiar music, old photos, simple tasks, gentle company. Engagement slows the slide.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  Sudden confusion (over days) is usually a treatable illness — treat it as urgent and medical. Slow confusion (over months and years) is more likely dementia. Knowing which changes everything you do.
DIGNITY  A confused elder is still a person, still feels fear and love and shame. Never mock, never talk about them as if absent, never restrain harshly. Meet confusion with calm and patience, not correction.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  Reversible causes have been checked at the PHC, the home is safe and the elder has ID on them, a calm routine is in place, and the family is supported and taught how to cope.

17
Loneliness & Bringing an Elder Back into Life
Reconnecting an elder who has become isolated — because for an old person, being unseen and unspoken-to for days is a real harm to body and mind, not just a sad mood.
WHEN YOU DO IT  When the elder is often lonely (Q15b = 2) or severely isolated (Q15b = 3) — going days without meaningful conversation.
How to do it
	•	First, tell loneliness apart from solitude. Some elders are content alone and do not want ‘fixing.’ Ask them. Push only where there is real, painful disconnection.
	•	Bring them into the centre — the single best tool. Meals, company, activity, other elders. Arrange the way there if walking is hard.
	•	Use the community kitchen as daily human contact — the delivery is a face and a few words every day.
	•	Pair them with a peer mentor (chapter 25) — another elder who visits, talks, includes them.
	•	Talk to the family about visiting and calling — sometimes children far away do not realise how much a weekly call means, or how silent the house has become.
	•	For a bedridden elder who cannot come out, bring life to them — a volunteer or neighbour who sits, a radio, their bed moved to where people pass.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  Loneliness can look like ‘just old age’ or be dismissed as the elder being difficult. It is neither — it genuinely harms health. Take it as seriously as a physical problem.
DIGNITY  Do not force company on someone at peace alone, and do not treat a lonely elder as pathetic. Offer connection as something good on its own, an elder among equals — not charity for the abandoned.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  The elder has regular, real human contact — through the centre, the kitchen, a peer mentor, or family — and the painful isolation has genuinely eased, by their own account.

E.  Safety
18
When You Suspect Abuse or Neglect
Protecting an elder you believe is being harmed, neglected, or exploited — carefully, because done wrong it can put them in more danger.
WHEN YOU DO IT  Any sign of abuse, neglect, abandonment, or exploitation (Q16 = 1 mild suspicion, 2 repeated, or 3 severe). The more serious, the faster and higher it goes.
How to do it
	•	Tell your supervisor the same day. You do NOT handle abuse alone — this is the firmest rule in this manual. Serious cases go to the supervisor and City Lead.
	•	Keep what you see in the restricted file only. Do not discuss it with neighbours, with other family, or with the suspected person.
	•	Do not confront the suspected abuser. Confrontation can make them punish the elder after you leave.
	•	Talk to the elder gently and privately, away from the household, at their pace. Do not push them to accuse anyone — let them say what they can.
	•	For serious or immediate danger, the supervisor and City Lead activate the helplines (Women 181, Elder 14567, Police 100) and arrange safe placement if the elder is at risk now.
	•	A safety plan is made (by the supervisor and lead) within 72 hours: who the elder can turn to, more frequent visits, how to protect them from the abuser learning they spoke.
	•	Keep watching closely — weekly — until the case is genuinely stable. De-escalating needs the supervisor’s agreement, not yours alone.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  What looks like ‘abandonment’ is often a family drowning in a tiny home with no money and no rest — exhaustion, not cruelty. Real abuse also exists. Do not judge from the doorway; observe, and let the supervisor help weigh it. Either way, the elder’s safety comes first.
DIGNITY  The elder may love and depend on the person hurting them, and may not want them punished. Respect that this is complicated for them. Protect them without forcing them to destroy their own family ties unless safety demands it.
DO NOT  Do not act alone, do not confront the abuser, do not disclose to the household that the elder spoke, and do not promise the elder things you cannot guarantee. Wrong moves here cause real harm.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  The supervisor and (for serious cases) City Lead are involved, a safety plan exists, the elder is safe, the record is restricted, and close monitoring is in place.

19
Unsafe Housing & Homelessness
Making an elder’s shelter safe enough to live in, or finding shelter when they have none.
WHEN YOU DO IT  When housing has moderate risks (Q17 = 2) or is dangerous, or the elder faces homelessness (Q17 = 3).
How to do it
	•	Look at the real dangers: bad wiring and fire risk, no ventilation, a roof or wall that could fall, flooding in monsoon, no safe place to cook, no light.
	•	Fix the cheap, fast safety things — lighting, a safer cooking spot, clearing a fire hazard.
	•	For structural problems, talk to the settlement leader and the supervisor — some need community or municipal action beyond what you can do.
	•	If the elder is homeless or the home is genuinely unliveable, raise it urgently with the supervisor; explore shelter options and any housing scheme they qualify for through the Entitlements Team.
	•	Watch for the property angle — an elder pushed out of their own home by relatives is both a housing and an abuse matter (chapter 18). Handle accordingly.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  Monsoon turns a marginal home dangerous — flooding, collapse, damp, illness. Check frail elders’ homes before the rains, not after.
DIGNITY  It is their home, however poor — made with their own years. Do not speak of it with disgust. Improve it with respect for what it means to them.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  The immediate dangers are fixed, structural issues are with the settlement leader and supervisor, and a homeless elder has a real shelter plan in motion.

F.  Food & Money
20
Enrolling an Elder in the Community Kitchen
Getting a hot daily meal to an elder who cannot cook or is not eating enough — and using that daily delivery as a daily check that they are alright.
WHEN YOU DO IT  When the elder cannot cook (Q8b), eats irregularly (Q18b = 2), faces food insecurity (Q18b = 3), or is home-bound and alone.
How to do it
	•	Confirm the real food situation: are meals missing, or coming but poor? Who cooks now? Why has it broken down? This decides whether the kitchen is the answer.
	•	Enrol the elder with the Kitchen In-charge — add them to the delivery list for their settlement.
	•	For a home-bound elder, daily delivery to the home; for a mobile elder, they can come to the centre for the meal and the company.
	•	Make sure the food suits them — soft food if they cannot chew (chapter 22), and culturally familiar, not strange to them.
	•	Use the delivery as a daily wellness check — brief the kitchen women to notice and report if the elder does not come to the door, seems unwell, or something has changed.
	•	Check the meal is actually being eaten and is enough — not given away to grandchildren or skipped.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  Sometimes the elder’s food goes to feed the family’s children — the elder quietly goes without so the young eat. Watch for this; it means the household is food-insecure and needs more than one elder’s meal solved.
DIGNITY  A shared meal is connection, not a handout. Frame the kitchen as the community feeding its elders — which is what it is — not charity for the destitute.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  The elder reliably gets an adequate daily meal they can eat, the kitchen women are watching over them daily, and you have confirmed the food is actually eaten.

21
Emergency Food / Dry Ration
Getting food into the house FAST when an elder has none — the stop-gap before the kitchen or entitlements catch up.
WHEN YOU DO IT  Severe food insecurity (Q18b = 3) or severe undernutrition (Q18a = 3) — act within 48 hours. This is an emergency, not a routine task.
How to do it
	•	Get a dry ration kit to the home within two days — do not let an elder go hungry while longer solutions are arranged.
	•	Start the community kitchen daily meal at the same time (chapter 20).
	•	Refer the ration-card gap to the Entitlements Team (chapter 23) so the long-term entitlement is fixed, not just the emergency.
	•	If the elder is severely wasted, this is also a medical matter — link the PHC to rule out an underlying illness (chapter 22).
	•	Check back within days — emergencies need eyes on them until stable.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  An elder who is severely wasted has often been quietly hungry for a long time — they rarely ask. By the time it shows, it is urgent. Move fast and do not wait for the next scheduled visit.
DIGNITY  Deliver emergency food quietly and respectfully, not as a public spectacle of someone’s destitution. Protect their standing in front of neighbours.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  Food reached the home within 48 hours, the daily kitchen meal has started, the ration-card gap is referred, and any medical cause is being checked.

22
When an Elder Cannot Eat Well — Nutrition, Soft Food, Teeth
Making sure an elder is actually nourished — not just fed — by solving the reasons they cannot eat properly.
WHEN YOU DO IT  When the elder shows thinness, weight loss, or chewing difficulty (Q18a = 2), or is visibly wasting (Q18a = 3).
How to do it
	•	Look, do not just ask — thin arms, loose clothes, a face grown gaunt. Ask the family if clothes have got loose over recent months.
	•	Find out why they cannot eat: no teeth or painful teeth so they cannot chew; the food is too hard; no appetite; an illness eating them; or simply not enough food.
	•	For chewing trouble: get soft food made — talk to the cook (family or kitchen) about softer, mashable meals — and refer to the PHC for dental help.
	•	For no appetite or wasting despite food: link the PHC to look for an illness (TB, untreated diabetes, other) — wasting with enough food is a medical warning.
	•	For not enough food: solve the access problem — kitchen, ration, entitlements (chapters 20, 21, 23).
	•	Follow up — is the weight loss stopping, is the elder eating more? Nutrition is judged over weeks, by the body, not by one meal seen.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  ‘They are eating fine’ from the family can be true while the elder still wastes — because they cannot chew it, or an illness is taking it. Trust the body you see over the report you hear.
DIGNITY  Not being able to chew, or having no teeth, is quietly humiliating. Solve it practically and kindly, without making the elder feel diminished.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  You know why the elder was not eating well, you have fixed that reason (soft food, teeth, illness, or access), and the wasting has stopped and weight is steadying.

23
Referring Entitlements to the Entitlements Team
Handing a pension, ration card, or other entitlement gap to the separate Entitlements Team — and then tracking it so it does not vanish into a queue.
WHEN YOU DO IT  When the elder is not getting a pension or benefit they are entitled to (Q20 = 2 or 3), or any missing entitlement: pension, ration card, Aadhaar, bank account, senior citizen card, disability certificate, widow pension.
How to do it
	•	Identify the specific gap clearly — which entitlement, and what exactly is blocking it (no Aadhaar, no bank account, application stuck, never applied).
	•	Refer it to the Entitlements Team with the full details — the elder, the settlement, the entitlement, the known blocker, how urgent. A vague referral stalls; a precise one moves.
	•	Remember: the elderly team does NOT process entitlements — the Entitlements Team does. Your job is a clean handover and then tracking.
	•	Track it at every visit — ask the elder if it has come, check with the Entitlements Team. Do not assume it is moving.
	•	If it stays stuck beyond a month, raise it jointly with the supervisor and the Entitlements Team — do not let it sit silently for a year.
	•	When it comes through, confirm with the elder that the money or card actually reached them — not just that it was ‘approved.’
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  Entitlements get stuck for small, fixable reasons — a missing document, a name spelt differently across papers, a dormant bank account. The pension that is ‘approved’ but not in the account is not done. Track to the money, not the approval.
DIGNITY  A pension is the elder’s right, earned by a lifetime as a citizen — not a favour. Help them claim it as something owed to them. Frame it that way to them, too; it changes how they carry themselves.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  The gap is clearly referred to the Entitlements Team, you are tracking it, and — when it comes — you have confirmed the benefit actually reached the elder.

G.  Connection
24
Building an Emergency Contact Chain
Making sure that when something goes wrong for an elder — a fall, sudden illness, distress — there is a clear, known chain of who gets called, in order.
WHEN YOU DO IT  When the elder’s emergency support is uncertain (Q21 = 2) or absent (Q21 = 3). Do it for every frail elder, especially those living alone.
How to do it
	•	Identify who can be called in trouble: family, a close neighbour, the settlement leader, you. Write down names and phone numbers.
	•	Put them in order — who is called first, second, third. The first should be someone close by who will actually answer and act.
	•	Make sure each person knows they are on the list and agrees, and has the others’ numbers.
	•	Write the chain clearly and leave it in the home where it can be found — and make sure the elder and a neighbour both know where it is.
	•	Include the real emergency numbers: 108 for ambulance, your number, the supervisor’s.
	•	Test it — even a small real event shows whether the chain works. Fix the gaps you find.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  A contact chain written and forgotten is useless. The test is whether, at 2am when the elder falls, someone is actually reached and comes. Build it for that moment, and check it works.
DIGNITY  Building this with the elder — asking whom THEY trust — respects their judgement about their own people. Do not just assign contacts over their head.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  There is a written, ordered contact chain in the home, everyone on it knows and agrees, the elder and a neighbour know where it is, and it has been tested.

25
Making an Elder a Peer Mentor
Inviting an active, capable elder to support other elders — giving them purpose and role, while extending the programme’s reach through someone the community trusts.
WHEN YOU DO IT  For Stable and able elders who have time, mobility, and willingness. Especially good for an elder who is lonely but capable — being needed heals.
How to do it
	•	Spot the elders who are doing well and still have energy and warmth — the ones who naturally look out for others.
	•	Invite them, do not assign — frame it as their experience being valued, which it is. Many will be glad to matter again.
	•	Pair them with two or three more vulnerable elders nearby — to visit, talk, include, keep a friendly eye on.
	•	Brief them simply: be a friend and a regular visitor, and tell the CO if something is wrong with the elder they watch. They are not doing your job — they are a caring neighbour with a light role.
	•	Bring the mentors together at the centre now and then — to share, to feel part of something, to be thanked.
	•	Notice and honour their contribution. A mentor who feels appreciated continues; one taken for granted drifts away.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  Do not overload a willing mentor or turn them into unpaid staff. Keep the role light and joyful. The point is mutual — the mentor gains purpose as much as the mentored gains support.
DIGNITY  This is the chapter that most says what we believe: an old person is not a problem to be managed but a person with something to give. A mentor is proof that age is not only need — it is also wisdom, time, and care to offer.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  The elder is happily paired with two or three others, visiting and watching over them, connected to the other mentors, and visibly enjoying being useful again.

H.  The Last Stretch
26
Palliative Care & Dignity at the End of Life
Accompanying an elder who is nearing the end — not trying to cure, but easing pain, keeping them comfortable and respected, and supporting the family through it.
WHEN YOU DO IT  When an elder has a terminal or advanced illness, or is in irreversible decline. Handle with the supervisor; this is tender, serious work.
How to do it
	•	Confirm with the supervisor and a medical opinion that this is end-of-life care — the goal shifts from cure to comfort and dignity.
	•	Talk with the family gently — led by the supervisor or Centre In-charge: understand what they know, what the elder wants if they can say, and what comfort and dignity mean here.
	•	Refer to a palliative care partner if available — for pain relief and home-based comfort care. No elder should die in unmanaged pain.
	•	Set up comfort care: pain relief access, keeping them clean and turned (chapter 8), mouth and skin care, the things that ease the body.
	•	Support the caregiver heavily — this is exhausting and grief-filled. Watch their burnout (chapter 3) closely.
	•	Honour the elder’s wishes — where they want to be, who they want near, their faith and rituals. Do not impose interventions they do not want.
	•	After death, visit the family — a bereavement visit. Sit with them. Their grief matters, and your presence honours the elder you both knew.
IN THE SLUM, WATCH FOR  Families may not know death is near, or may not be able to speak of it. Move at their pace, with great gentleness. And watch your own heart — this work is heavy; debrief with your supervisor, you do not carry it alone.
DIGNITY  This is the deepest meaning of everything in this manual. We believe ageing is a completion, not only a decline — and the end of life is the final stretch of that road. We walk it beside the elder with respect, easing pain, never abandoning them, never treating dying as failure. We help them leave with dignity, among people who see them fully to the last.
YOU'RE DONE WHEN  The elder is comfortable and free of unmanaged pain, their wishes are honoured, the family is supported, and after death you have sat with the family in their grief.

A last word
These twenty-six chapters are not a rulebook to obey but a set of tools to use well. The steps tell you what to do; the spirit in which you do them is yours to bring. Look across at the elder, never down. Ask what they want before deciding what they need. Follow through until the thing is real. And remember that how you do a thing — the gentleness, the respect, the refusal to shame — matters as much as that you do it.
You will learn far more in the settlements than any manual can hold. When you discover a better way to do one of these things, bring it back so the next CO learns it too. This manual is meant to grow with what you learn.
— End of the CO Action Manual —
