Executive Note
This document accompanies the Elder Vulnerability Rapid Assessment Tool (EVRAT v4) and its Operational Guidelines. EVRAT identifies and categorises elders by vulnerability; this document specifies what the programme does in response.
Three operational principles run through the document:
	•	Categorisation must produce response. An elder labelled Tier 1 or Profound Need has the right to specific actions within specific timeframes. The category is a commitment, not a description.
	•	Response must be standardised but applied with judgement. Standard pathways are provided per domain; care plans are tailored to the elder. The programme exists in the space between the standard and the person.
	•	Programme delivery is layered, not heroic. A single Community Organiser cannot serve 500 elders at the cadences EVRAT defines. The operations model distributes responsibility across a Centre In-charge, Kitchen In-charge, Community Organisers, community kitchen women, and referral partners. The unit-cost calculator surfaces the resourcing implications for transparent decision-making.
Janadhikara is the donor and the implementer through partners. This document is the operating standard that partners are funded against and reviewed by. It is prescriptive about what must happen and permissive about how each partner organises to do it.

Part 1  •  Care Plan Derivation Logic
Every EVA item, at meaningful response levels, triggers a specific response with a priority, owner, and timeframe. This table converts assessment scores into action. It is the reference COs and supervisors use after every assessment to populate Section E2 of the EVRAT (Care Plan task table).
Notes on the table:
	•	Owner abbreviations: CO = Community Organiser; KI = Kitchen In-charge; CI = Centre In-charge; ET = Entitlements Team (separate from elderly team, by referral); Sup = Supervisor.
	•	Timeframes are maximum acceptable response times from assessment date.
	•	Priority: H = High (within 1 week); M = Medium (within 1 month); L = Low (opportunistic).
	•	Multiple triggers may produce overlapping tasks; the CO consolidates and avoids duplication when populating Section E2.
	•	All entitlement-related responses are referrals to the Entitlements Team; the elderly team does not process entitlements directly.

3.1  Profound Need — Template Care Plan
Profound Need is the most intensive tier. Either the elder has crossed the weighted-score threshold (≥84) or has at least one critical risk flag. The care plan presumes immediacy, intensity, and frequent reassessment.
Visit pattern
	•	2–3 home visits per week by CO (one of which may be a brief well-being check ~15 minutes; one a full visit ~45 minutes).
	•	Daily food delivery via community kitchen, with at least one delivery acting as a wellness check (kitchen women report changes to CO).
	•	Weekly supervisor review of the case in CO weekly huddle.
	•	Monthly family meeting to align caregiving.
Core components (always present)
	•	Caregiver protocol — primary and backup caregiver identified, trained on the specific elder's needs (bed sore prevention, transfer, medication, comfort).
	•	PHC home-visit linkage — ANM or doctor visits monthly minimum; supplies coordinated.
	•	Mobility / continence support — wheelchair/commode/diapers stocked; air mattress if at bed sore risk.
	•	Nutrition support — daily community kitchen meal; soft food adaptation if needed.
	•	Emergency response chain — written plan, all caregivers and CO contacts known to family, neighbour, settlement leader.
	•	Restricted-access case file — sensitive observations (mental health, abuse, palliative) stored separately, supervisor + city lead access only.
Variable components (added based on EVA findings)
	•	If palliative flag: dignity-in-dying plan, palliative partner referral, pain relief access, bereavement protocol on hand.
	•	If severe abuse flag: see Part 5 abuse protocol — case migrates to Sup + City Lead ownership.
	•	If severe depression: weekly mental health check; counsellor visits; self-harm risk monitored.
	•	If no dependable caregiver: community caregiver arrangement (paid neighbour); intensified CO visits act as partial substitute.
Reassessment cadence
	•	Full EVRAT re-run monthly.
	•	Event-triggered re-assessment within 48 hours of any fall, hospitalisation, caregiver death, or visible deterioration.
	•	Supervisor validation required to de-escalate to High Need; sustained 8 weeks of stability minimum.
Expected outcomes at 6 months
	•	Bed sores prevented or healing where present.
	•	Nutrition status (Q18a) at 1 or lower.
	•	No medication interruption beyond 48 hours at any point.
	•	Caregiver burden (Q6b) at 2 or lower with respite arrangement.
	•	Dignity preserved through every intervention.
3.2  High Need — Template Care Plan
High Need elders have multiple vulnerabilities but are usually mobile or partially mobile. The care plan addresses each vulnerability with structured response and monitors closely.
Visit pattern
	•	Fortnightly home visit by CO (full visit ~30 minutes).
	•	Weekly community kitchen contact (delivery or in-person visit if mobile).
	•	Monthly centre visit encouraged if mobility permits.
	•	Fortnightly supervisor review of the case.
Core components
	•	Caregiver identified and supported (if present); caregiver burden assessed monthly.
	•	PHC/UPHC enrolment and regular follow-up; medication adherence monitored.
	•	Mobility aid procured if needed; home hazards addressed.
	•	Community kitchen enrolment if food access affected; centre attendance if mobile.
	•	Emergency contact chain in place.
	•	Entitlements team referral active for any missing benefit.
Variable components
	•	Falls history: confidence-building, home modifications, fall prevention counselling.
	•	NCD load: structured medication review; quarterly clinical follow-up.
	•	Sensory loss: eye/ENT camp referral; disability certificate workflow.
	•	Mental health: counselling referral; centre attendance for engagement.
Reassessment cadence
	•	Full EVRAT re-run quarterly.
	•	Event-triggered re-assessment within 7 days of any deterioration event.
	•	Movement to Moderate Need possible after 12 weeks of stable improvement.
3.3  Moderate Need — Template Care Plan
Moderate Need elders have identifiable vulnerabilities but are managing day-to-day. The plan focuses on closing specific gaps and preventing deterioration.
Visit pattern
	•	Monthly home visit by CO (full visit ~25 minutes).
	•	Centre attendance recommended (twice weekly minimum) if mobile.
	•	Community kitchen if food access requires it.
	•	Quarterly supervisor review of the case.
Core components
	•	PHC linkage if NCDs present; medication routine confirmed.
	•	Mobility aids procured if needed.
	•	Centre engagement for social connection and meal.
	•	Emergency contact known.
	•	Entitlements referrals on any missing benefit.
Reassessment cadence
	•	Full EVRAT re-run quarterly.
	•	Event-triggered immediate reassessment.
	•	Movement to Low Need after 6 months of sustained improvement.
3.4  Low Need — Template Care Plan
Low Need elders are largely managing well with minor gaps. The plan keeps a light touch but maintains relationship.
Visit pattern
	•	Quarterly home visit by CO (~20 minutes).
	•	Centre attendance optional but encouraged.
	•	Annual supervisor review.
Core components
	•	Identification of any unaddressed entitlement gap.
	•	Encouragement to participate in centre activities.
	•	Preventive health awareness (NCDs, falls, mental health).
	•	Emergency contact verified annually.
Reassessment cadence
	•	Full EVRAT re-run twice yearly.
	•	Event-triggered escalation.
	•	Movement to Stable possible after 12 months of stability.
3.5  Stable & Self-Managing — Template Care Plan
Stable elders are functioning well. The programme's role is light-touch monitoring, preventive engagement, and peer mentorship enrolment for those willing.
Visit pattern
	•	3-monthly home visit or centre contact (~15 minutes).
	•	Centre engagement encouraged; potential peer mentor role for other elders.
Core components
	•	Light wellness check; IVS only (unless trigger detected).
	•	Peer mentor enrolment if elder is willing — pairs with 2–3 Tier 2 or 3 elders.
	•	Annual entitlements check.
	•	Preventive health awareness.
Reassessment cadence
	•	Full EVRAT re-run annually unless event triggers earlier.
	•	Status reviewed every 3 months — Stable cannot remain unreviewed beyond 3 months (per EVRAT Guidelines).
Stable elders are an asset to the programme. Their lived experience, mobility, and time make them ideal peer mentors and community contacts for less able elders. A flourishing peer mentor cohort reduces CO load significantly and dignifies the Stable cohort with purpose.

Part 4  •  A Year in Three Elders
Three composite elders are tracked month by month through Year 1 of the programme. Names are illustrative; situations are typical of slum-elder populations in Bangalore and Chennai. The intent is to show how the framework applies in messy reality — including things that go wrong, change, and have to be adjusted.
The three elders are deliberately chosen to span the tier system: one Profound Need (Lakshmamma), one Moderate Need (Sarojamma), and one Stable transitioning to Low Need (Muniyappa).
4.1  Lakshmamma — Tier 1, Profound Need
Profile at baseline (April):
	•	78 years old, female, lives alone in a single-room dwelling in JBN settlement, Bangalore.
	•	Widow for 12 years; one daughter married and living in another city, visits twice yearly.
	•	BP and Sugar diagnosed but irregular medication due to cost; one fall in last 6 months; visible thinness; cataract in right eye; widow-pension applied 2 years ago, never received.
	•	EVA total: 91 (Profound Need); critical flag: Severe untreated vision loss; widow-property pressure noted in Q16.

At ≈287 visits per week × 50 minutes per visit = 239 CO-hours of visit work per week. Add roughly 20% for case notes, team meetings, kitchen liaison, and unscheduled events = 287 hours/week of elderly-team work.
A single CO realistically delivers about 30 productive hours per week (accounting for leave, training, meetings, travel between sites). At this rate, 287 hours / 30 hours = approximately 9–10 COs needed for 500 elders at the EVRAT-defined cadences.
The current budget funds 1 CO. The gap is structural — not a matter of effort, but of arithmetic.
Three response scenarios
There are three honest ways to close this gap. The calculator implements all three so the donor-implementer team can model and choose.
Scenario
Approach
Implication on unit cost
Scenario A — Honour the cadences as defined
Fund 8–10 COs per 500-elder catchment. Maintain visit frequencies per EVRAT.
Programme delivery cost rises significantly (≈ 4× current budget for staffing). Per-catchment-elder unit cost rises from ₹6,530/year to roughly ₹25,000–₹30,000/year.
Scenario B — Soften the cadences
Reduce Profound from 2–3×/week to weekly; High from fortnightly to monthly; Moderate to quarterly. Maintains 1–2 COs per centre.
Unit cost stays near current. Risk: programme commitments in EVRAT Guidelines Section 17 are not honoured. Quality of Profound Need response weakens.
Scenario C — Hybrid with community volunteers and peer mentors
Funded COs handle Profound + High + first-contact Moderate. Stable peers and trained settlement volunteers handle Low + Stable check-ins under CO supervision. Kitchen women act as daily check for Profound.
Unit cost moderate. Requires investment in volunteer training, peer mentor honorarium, and supervisory structure for non-staff actors. CO count ~3–4 per 500-elder catchment.
Janadhikara has stated that money is not the constraint; unit cost is. The decision is therefore not 'can we afford it' but 'what unit cost is defensible for the depth of care committed in EVRAT.' Scenarios A and C produce defensible cost-per-elder figures that fund the care promised. Scenario B reduces cost by reducing care. The calculator allows the team to test each.
Centre and Kitchen In-charge load
The Centre In-charge oversees one centre serving ~30–60 daily attendees, coordinates the team, manages caregiver groups, and handles walk-ins. At 30–60 attendees daily plus team coordination, the 1-Centre-In-charge + 1-Helper model is feasible if the centre's scope stays at daily activities, not entitlement processing or clinical work.
The Kitchen In-charge oversees 3–5 community kitchens. At weekly inspection per kitchen + supply coordination + payments + quality issues, this is roughly 25 hours/week of work — feasible as a full role. Caution: if kitchens scale beyond 5, a second Kitchen In-charge becomes necessary.

Closing Note
This operations document, the EVRAT assessment instrument and operational guidelines (v4 redrafts), and the Resourcing & Unit Cost Calculator together form the programme operating standard for Janadhikara's Elderly Care Programme across Bangalore and Chennai.
The documents are designed to be living: revised annually based on field learning, calibration findings, and outcome data. Changes are reviewed by the donor-implementer team in consultation with partner organisations and, where possible, with elder representatives themselves.
The core commitment is this: vulnerability identified is vulnerability acted on, within timeframes that honour the dignity of the elder and the realities of the field. The categorisation system, the care plan logic, the partner ecosystem, and the resourcing math exist to make that commitment operational and reviewable.
