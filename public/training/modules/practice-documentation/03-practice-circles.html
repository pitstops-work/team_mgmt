Module 3 · 150 minutes

Practice Circles — Composition, Cadence, Run-of-Show

Owner CO RP The densest module · re-read before your first circle

A practice circle is the single biggest source of edits to a playbook. It is also the easiest thing in this curriculum to get wrong — circles drift into status meetings, owners monologue, the same dramatic case gets re-told for months, and the page never changes. This module is the operating protocol. Hold the discipline; it works.

3.1What a practice circle is for

A practice circle is a 90-minute monthly convening of community organisers doing the same kind of work at different partner organisations, with the page owner present, to compare how the work is actually done and to surface concrete edits to the playbook.

It is not a status meeting. It is not a training. It is not a venting session. It is not for the owner to lecture about the playbook. It is a structured space where the field comes into the room, gets compared across partners, and leaves as written changes to a page.

The output test

Every circle produces, at minimum: one concrete page edit, two open questions for the next cycle, and a recorded case discussed. If a circle produces no edits, something went wrong — either the case was too easy, the wrong people came, or the owner did all the talking. Name it. Do not paper over it in the minutes.

3.2Composition — who is in the room

The default for every domain, in every city, is the same: cross-partner. A circle is scoped by vertical (food, sanitation, creche, etc.) and city, not by partner. COs from multiple partner NGOs doing the same kind of work sit together.

Cross-partner — the default

  • 5 to 6 COs from different partners.
  • Comparison is the point: "how does your partner do this?" is the most useful question in the room.
  • Diluted partner-specific reflexes — fewer "we have always done it this way" loops.
  • Cross-partner trust grows as a side effect.

In-partner — only when

  • A single partner has 4+ COs in one domain — add an in-partner circle in addition, not instead.
  • A partner-specific practice (their crèche curriculum, their kitchen menu) needs its own forum.
  • Never replace the cross-partner circle with an in-partner one.

Size rules

  • Below 4 attendees: cancel and merge with the next month. A 3-person circle is a coffee chat.
  • 4 to 7: the working range. Everyone speaks; comparison stays sharp.
  • Above 7: split into two parallel circles. People stop speaking past 7.

Who attends besides COs

The owner is present by default — as a participant and scribe, not as the centre of the room. The RP for that domain attends. The ZL does not attend regularly — they read the minutes. A curator may sit in once a quarter for cross-page hygiene checks.

3.3Cadence — when circles run

The default cadence is monthly, third week of the month. The first week is pitstop-day pressure; the last week is month-close. The third week gives breathing room on both sides.

Domain velocityCadenceWhy
High — daily ops (food, water)MonthlyIssues compound fast; the playbook needs to keep up.
Medium — weekly ops (creche, CLC, sanitation)MonthlyDefault; balances effort and signal.
Low — episodic (welfare rights, scheme drives)QuarterlyMonthly would be empty rooms.
Cancel-do-not-postpone

If a circle cannot happen on its planned date, it is cancelled for that cycle — not postponed. Once you postpone once, you postpone forever. The circle reconvenes on the next month's planned date. This is uncomfortable; it is also the only rule that holds the calendar.

3.4Facilitation — owner for 3 months, then rotation

The single most important protocol rule. A new owner facilitates the first 3 circles of their term, then facilitation rotates among the CO attendees.

Why the owner facilitates first

A new owner needs to see the room to delegate it. They learn which COs speak, which hold back, which dominate, which case-types repeat, what language the room defaults to. Three months is enough to read the room; longer is the owner taking up space that should belong to the COs.

Why rotation after

If the owner facilitates forever, the circle becomes a status meeting. COs report up; they do not compare across. Rotation also doubles as a development pipeline — the COs who facilitate well are the ones to watch for future owner roles.

The cold-start clock resets per domain

"Three months of owner facilitation" is counted from circles 1, 2, and 3 of a given (vertical, city, owner) — not from a calendar quarter. If you take over a long-running domain at month 4, you still get circles 1–3 to facilitate. If a new vertical opens, the same rule applies fresh.

After rotation — who facilitates next month

  • Facilitator for next month is named at the end of this month's circle. Not "we'll see" — a specific person.
  • The owner sends the facilitator a one-page brief 48 hours before: last circle's open questions, this circle's case shortlist, two pages whose review is overdue.
  • The owner is permanently accountable for the circle happening on the day it said it would. Facilitator runs the 90 minutes; owner keeps the calendar.

3.5Language — vernacular by default

Circles run in the language the COs speak best — Kannada in Bangalore, Tamil in Chennai. Not the language the owner is most comfortable in. Not English by reflex when a senior person is in the room.

Recording is in vernacular. The owner's summary (the basis of page edits) is drafted in vernacular first. The translation pipeline produces the English version, not the other way around. Pages translated from vernacular read better than pages translated into vernacular — and the COs know which is which.

The vernacular collapse

The most common failure: a senior person walks in mid-circle and the language switches to English to accommodate them. The energy in the room drops; the COs stop comparing and start performing. If you see this happen, switch back to the vernacular within thirty seconds, even if the senior person is left out. They can read the minutes.

3.6The pre-read — one page, sent 48 hours ahead

Every circle has a one-page pre-read. Sent 48 hours before. Read aloud at the start for anyone who did not get to it.

  • Open questions from the last circle — three to four, no more.
  • Two cases from the field this month — anonymised, real, drawn from pitstops, partner reviews, or shadow visits.
  • One page due for review — pre-selected by the owner. Attendees should skim before walking in.

If the pre-read takes more than one page, you are over-preparing. The point is to walk in oriented, not briefed.

3.7The run-of-show — 90 minutes

The structure is fixed. Hard stop at 90 minutes. "One more thing" is never one more thing.

00:00
Open round — every attendee, one sentence: "one thing that surprised me this month." 10 minutes.
10:00
Case 1. The facilitator walks the case; attendees compare to their own practice; the owner scribes. 30 minutes.
40:00
Case 2, or selected page walkthrough. Same shape. 30 minutes.
70:00
Synthesis. Facilitator names the proposed page edits in plain language; attendees agree, disagree, or sharpen. Open questions for next time named explicitly. 15 minutes.
85:00
Close. Next facilitator named. Next month's case shortlist agreed. Date confirmed. 5 minutes.

The open round is non-negotiable

It feels soft. It is the most important ten minutes. The "one thing that surprised me" answers seed every case discussion that follows. Skip it, and the circle defaults to whatever the most senior or most vocal person decided to discuss before walking in.

3.8Capture — scribe is not facilitator

The facilitator runs the room. They cannot also write down what is being said. The owner scribes — or, when the owner is not present (rare, see below), a designated CO scribes.

  • Raw notes land in the circle record: attendees, case discussed, language, notes, optional recording URL.
  • Page-edit deltas are drafted by the owner within 48 hours — Module 2's drafting protocol applies.
  • Draft circulates back to circle attendees for line-edit feedback before publish.
  • Linked-pages on the circle record is the contract: every circle must touch at least one page. If linkedPages = 0, the circle was a status meeting.

3.9Power dynamics — when the owner should not be in the room

If the owner is senior to every CO in attendance, attendees often will not say "the playbook is wrong." Two responses, used selectively.

Every fourth circle without the owner

Once a quarter, the circle runs with only COs and the facilitator. The facilitator writes up the unfiltered version. The owner reads it the next day and incorporates the edits. This is uncomfortable for the owner; it is also where the most honest edits come from.

Anonymous flag channel

A CO who cannot raise a concern in the room can file a flag against the page itself. The flag goes into the next circle's pre-read, anonymised. The owner addresses it as a case.

The "yes sir" smell test

If your circle minutes are all consensus and no friction, something is being eaten. Politely-held disagreement is the sound of a healthy circle. If you have not had a disagreement in three circles, the room is performing for the owner — not comparing across partners.

3.11Health metrics — the dashboard for circles

The wiki dashboard surfaces five numbers per circle series. The ZL watches them; the owner is accountable for them.

  • Attendance vs invited — target ≥ 70%. Below 50% for two cycles is a red flag.
  • Page edits per circle — target ≥ 1. Three consecutive zeroes is a sunset trigger (see below).
  • Median days from circle to page edit — target ≤ 2. Past 4 days, the 48-hour SLA is breaking.
  • Circle survival — how many circles in the last 6 months out of how many should have run. Below 50% means the calendar is not being held.
  • Facilitator rotation index — number of unique facilitators in the last 6 months. After the owner's first 3 months, this should be ≥ 3.

3.12Sunset — when to stop a circle

Not every circle should run forever. A circle that no longer produces value should be paused or dropped, not kept on the calendar to look good.

  • Three consecutive circles with zero page edits → pause. Reconvene only when a specific case arises.
  • The domain has not changed in 12 months → drop from monthly to quarterly.
  • Attendance below 50% for two cycles → ask the COs why. The answer is usually "we no longer find it useful" — listen to that.
A paused circle is not a failure

Practices stabilise. A circle that paused after 18 months because the playbook is now mature is a success story, not a defeat. Record the sunset on the wiki — the next person to pick up this domain should know why the circle stopped, so they can restart it on a real trigger.

3.13What circles are NOT for

Pull the wrong content into a circle and the circle stops working.

  • Principles. Do not relitigate principle pages in a CO circle. Principles are reviewed quarterly by the principle owner, not by the room.
  • Tech / app bugs. Different forum — file an issue, not a flag.
  • Conflict between partners. Different forum — the partner review meeting (Module 4) or leadership intervention.
  • Personal performance feedback. The circle is about practice, not about a specific person's work.
Check your understanding

Five questions — answer before you move on

  1. You are a new owner. You facilitated circles 1 and 2. Circle 3 is in two weeks and only three COs can attend. What do you do?
  2. It is month 5. The CO facilitator you scheduled for this month falls ill the morning of the circle. Six COs are en route. What is your sequence — postpone, cancel, take over, or run with the most senior CO in attendance? Why?
  3. A circle produces a clear page-edit proposal. The owner does not draft within 48 hours. What does this signal, and what should the ZL do?
  4. Your circle has had three consecutive sessions with strong attendance, lively discussion, and zero page edits. What is your diagnosis?
  5. A partner CO raises in the circle that the playbook is unrealistic for shelters with no kitchen on site. Three other COs disagree. The owner is mid-monologue. Name the three protocol corrections you would make in this moment, in order.
