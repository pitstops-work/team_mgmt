Module 4 · 75 minutes

Partner Review Meetings — Quarterly with Partner Leadership

Owner RP ZL For owners and RPs running partner-facing pages

A practice circle catches what the field is doing. A partner review meeting catches what the partner organisation has decided. The two are different — partners change policy, hire, restructure, or quietly shift their model, and the playbook needs to reflect this. The partner review meeting is the quarterly forum where these shifts surface.

4.1What it is, what it is not

A partner review meeting is a 90-minute quarterly session with the senior leadership of one partner NGO, attended by the relevant owner(s), an RP, and (optionally) a ZL. Its purpose is to surface partner-level changes to practice and convert them into wiki edits.

It is

  • Practice-level: how the partner runs its programmes.
  • Honest: partner says "we changed X" without fear of audit consequences.
  • Triggering: every shift surfaced becomes a wiki edit or a flag.
  • Per partner, per quarter — even if the partner is small.

It is not

  • A finance review. Money has its own quarterly meeting.
  • A contract negotiation.
  • A grievance forum for one CO's complaint.
  • A status report from us to them — both sides bring change.
Why this is separate from the practice circle

Practice circles are cross-partner and CO-level. Partner reviews are single-partner and leadership-level. A CO does not have the authority to confirm "yes, we have changed our model" on behalf of the partner — only senior leadership does. The partner review is where that confirmation happens.

Keep the room small. Six attendees max. A partner review with twelve people is a status meeting in disguise.

4.3Cadence and scheduling

Quarterly per partner. Not flexible. The partner relationship that does not hold a quarterly cadence is the one whose practice quietly drifts.

  • Booked one year in advance. Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4 — dates fixed at the start of the calendar year. Partner leadership knows these are on their calendar.
  • Scheduling is the RP's job — not the partner's. We hold the rhythm; we do not wait to be invited.
  • In person where geography allows. Remote when not. Hybrid is fine if the partner is comfortable.
  • If a meeting cannot happen, it cancels for the quarter — same rule as circles. No postponement that becomes a postponed-forever.

4.4The pre-read — two pages, both sides

Sent 48 hours before. One page from us, one from the partner. If only one side prepares, the meeting is asymmetric and the asymmetric side dominates.

Our pre-read (one page)

  • Open flags and gaps tagged to this partner — short list.
  • Things we have observed in shadow visits or pitstop runs this quarter that may indicate a shift.
  • One or two specific questions we want clean answers on. Not ten.

Partner pre-read (one page)

  • What has shifted in their model or practice this quarter — hiring, restructuring, new methods, dropped methods.
  • What they want us to know. Their version of "things we have observed."
  • One or two asks of us — if any. Not a wish list.
If the partner does not send a pre-read

Do not cover for them. Do not write their side ourselves. Show up with our page only, and ask their answers verbally in the meeting. Politely. The pattern of "we always write both sides" makes the meeting one-sided forever.

The "practice changes noted" capture

The single most important note from a partner review is the answer to one question: "What has changed in your practice this quarter that the playbook should now reflect?" This goes into the partner-review record verbatim, in the partner's own words. It is the basis of the draft edits the owner will make in the 48 hours that follow.

4.6Beneficiary feedback — the second source

A partner review is staff-facing. Beneficiaries — the households and individuals the partner serves — see the practice from the other side. Once a quarter, alongside the partner review, light-touch beneficiary feedback enters the same conversation.

  • Five household conversations per active programme. The owner or RP runs them. Not a survey — conversations.
  • Notes brought to the partner review, anonymised. "Three of five mothers said the centre opens late on Mondays" — concrete.
  • Partner is asked to respond, not to defend. The point is what the playbook should now say.
Why this is folded in here

Beneficiary feedback that arrives without a forum gets filed and forgotten. Folding it into the partner review gives it a place where someone in the room has the authority to act on it.

4.7From meeting to page edit

Same 48-hour SLA as a practice circle. The owner drafts edits to every linked page within 48 hours of the partner review. The version note links back to the partner review record.

  • Record the partner review: org, scheduled date, attendees, practice changes noted (verbatim), linked pages.
  • Within 48 hours: draft page edits triggered by the meeting; circulate to attendees from our side.
  • Within 7 days: partner leadership sees the draft before it publishes — quick courtesy review, not approval.
  • Publish with a version note that links the partner review ID.

Check your understanding

Three questions — answer before you move on

  1. A partner says in the review: "we have stopped doing the monthly nutrition check; the Anganwadi covers it now." Three of your wiki pages assume the partner does this check. What is your sequence in the next 48 hours?
  2. The partner sends no pre-read for the second quarter in a row. What do you do — write theirs, cancel the meeting, escalate to leadership, or show up with only our side and ask verbally? Why?
  3. Beneficiary feedback at three of five households says the creche teacher shouts at children. The partner pushes back hard, denies it. What goes into the playbook this quarter, and what does not?
