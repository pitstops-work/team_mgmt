Module 0 · 60 minutes

Induction — Why We Document Practice

All audiences Foundation · run this for the whole intake together

Every domain we run has practices that live in someone's head — how a CO walks a settlement, how an owner closes a centre's monthly review, how a kitchen reconciles wastage. When that person leaves, the practice leaves with them. The wiki exists so practice survives a handover. This module sets up why we document, who does what, and what the rest of this curriculum will teach.

0.1The problem we are solving

Most NGO knowledge is lost the day a senior CO retires, a programme manager moves, or a partner shifts focus. The next person walks in, asks "how do we do X here?", and gets five different answers. Months of relearning follow. Sometimes the practice never returns to its earlier quality. We have watched this happen to good programmes, twice.

The wiki is the antidote. It is the place where a CO can open one page, read how the practice is actually done at this partner, in this domain, and start doing it correctly on day one. It is the place where an owner records what changed and why, so the next owner does not have to re-discover it. It is the place where a flag from the field travels to the desk of the person who can fix it.

The wiki is not a manual

A manual is written once and goes stale. The wiki is rewritten continuously — every practice circle, every partner review, every field shadow visit feeds it. The pages do not aspire to be perfect; they aspire to be current. A current page that is 80% right beats a perfect page that is 18 months old.

0.2The five players

Five people touch a wiki page. Know which one you are before you do anything else.

RoleWhat they do on the wikiWhere they sit
Owner Owns a single page. Drafts edits, runs the quarterly review, accepts handover. 6-month term. Partner senior staff or our RP, depending on domain.
CO Reads the page. Flags errors. Files gaps. Attends practice circles. Brings the field into the room. Partner field staff — the people doing the work.
RP Resource Person. Owns pages for our directly-run programmes; attends circles as a participant for partner-owned pages. Our staff.
ZL Zonal Leader. Watches the dashboard. Notices stale pages, missed circles, gaps that haven't moved in a month. Intervenes. Our staff.
Curator Cross-page consistency. Triages flags and comments weekly. Walks the gap queue. One per city. Designated, not elected.

Owner is the only role with editorial authority over a specific page. Everyone else feeds the owner — through circles, flags, comments, gaps, and observations. The curator can intervene only on cross-page hygiene, not content.

0.3The water sources — how a page stays alive

A page only stays useful if multiple streams keep flowing into it. If even one dries up, the page rots quietly.

  • Practice circles — monthly convening of COs across partners in one domain. The single biggest source of edits to playbooks.
  • Partner review meetings — quarterly with partner leadership. Surfaces "we have quietly changed how we do X."
  • Field shadow visits — owner walks alongside a CO doing the documented activity. Catches drift the office never sees.
  • CO onboarding sessions — every new CO is walked through the playbook by the owner. Their "I don't get X" questions are a documentation-gap goldmine.
  • Practice gap queue — one-line "there is no page for the thing I am doing today" notes filed from the field, triaged weekly.
  • Flags & comments — reader-raised "this page is wrong" or "I need clarification."
  • Quarterly owner review — the owner walks the page on a 90-day cadence, even if nothing else happened.
  • Owner handover — every 6 months. Incoming owner does a listen-tour before accepting.

Modules 3 through 8 of this curriculum teach each of these streams. Module 9 is the trainer's guide.

0.4The non-negotiables — every audience, every page

Hard rules

A page has a single owner. Not a committee, not a partner organisation — one named person. If a page has no owner, it does not get published.

An owner term is 6 months. Renewable, but never automatic. The handover step is mandatory even if the owner stays.

Every page edit links back to its trigger — a circle, a partner review, a flag, a gap, a shadow visit, a quarterly review. An edit untraceable to a trigger is suspicious.

A flag or comment is acknowledged within seven days. Resolved or explicitly declined within thirty.

A page that has not been reviewed in 90 days is flagged stale on the dashboard. Past 180 days it is marked orphaned until reviewed.

Practice circles run in the vernacular of the attendees. The translation pipeline produces the English version, not the other way around.

The one rule above all others

The wiki is downstream of the field. If the page disagrees with what good COs actually do today, the page is wrong, not the COs. The owner's job is to keep the page in lockstep with the practice — not to police the practice into matching the page.

Status language

The dashboard uses three words for every page and every trigger. Learn them.

GREEN reviewed in window, no open flags AMBER review due, or open flags < 30 days RED stale > 90 days, or open flags > 30 days

0.5What this module is, and what it is not

This module is

  • The operational protocol for keeping the wiki alive — circles, reviews, flags, handovers.
  • Mandatory for every wiki owner before their first term.
  • Mandatory for every RP and ZL within their first month.
  • Recommended for any CO who will attend practice circles regularly.

This module is not

  • The principles of how we work — those live as wiki principle pages.
  • A guide to using the wiki software (editor, comments UI, translation flags) — separate quick-reference.
  • Domain training — that is the food / water / sanitation modules.
  • An advocacy of "more documentation." It is the opposite: less, but kept current.
Check your understanding

Three questions — answer before you move on

  1. A CO at a partner runs an enrolment session in a way that disagrees with the published playbook. What is the right next step, and who takes it?
  2. You are an owner. Your 6-month term ends in two weeks. Name the three things that must happen before the handover is complete.
  3. What does AMBER on a page mean, and what is the latest acceptable response time before it becomes RED?
