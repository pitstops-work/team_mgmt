Module 7 · 60 minutes

Flags, Comments & Translation Flags — Reader-Raised Triage

Owner Curator CO The reactive channel · weekly cadence

Practice circles and partner reviews are convened. Shadow visits and onboarding sessions are scheduled. Flags, comments and translation flags are different — they arrive on the reader's schedule, from the field, asynchronously. If the response is slow, readers stop filing. This module is the protocol that keeps the channel alive.

7.1The three reader signals

SignalMeansRouted toSLA
Flag"This page is wrong, missing, or contradicted by practice."Page ownerAcknowledged in 7 days, resolved in 30.
Comment"I have a question, observation, or suggestion on this page."Page owner (visible to all readers)Responded in 14 days.
Translation flag"The translation of this page into <language> is wrong."Translation queue (city curator)Acknowledged in 7 days, fixed in 21.

All three are async. None of them should pull the owner into a conversation in the moment. The owner handles them on their weekly triage day; the curator on theirs.

7.2Flags — the protocol

When to file a flag

  • Wrong: the page contradicts what good COs actually do today.
  • Missing: the page has a gap that needs filling on this page (not a separate page — that is a gap).
  • Out of date: something external has changed and the page has not caught up.
  • Unclear: the page is technically correct but unusable in the field.

What a good flag looks like

A flag is two to four sentences. Concrete, with a section reference if possible.

A clean flag

"Section 3.2 of the creche playbook says the nutrition check is monthly. Since last quarter, three of four partners do it weekly because of the Anganwadi schedule. The page should be updated."

A poor flag

"This page is unclear." (No section reference, no detail, nothing for the owner to act on.)

Owner's weekly flag triage

  • Same day each week. 20 minutes for a healthy queue.
  • For each open flag: acknowledge with one line in the comment thread. Even "saw this, will respond in 14 days" counts.
  • Within 30 days: resolve — either edit the page (preferred), bring as a case to the next practice circle, or decline with a one-line reason.
  • A declined flag is rare and explained. "We considered this and the page is correct because X." Readers see the decline and the reason.

7.3Comments — softer than flags

A comment is a question, observation, or suggestion. It is not "the page is wrong." It is "I am reading this and wondering about X." Comments are visible to all readers; the discussion they spark is often more useful than the page edit they may produce.

  • Owner responds within 14 days. A response is enough — not every comment requires a page edit.
  • Threads stay open while the topic is live. They are resolved when the owner closes them with a summary line.
  • A comment can be promoted to a flag if the discussion reveals the page is actually wrong. The owner does the promotion.
Comments as community memory

Healthy comment threads are a sign of an engaged readership. A page with steady comments is being read; a page with no comments may be dead even if the version count is high. The dashboard shows comments-per-quarter as a vitality signal.

7.4Translation flags — per-language quality

Pages have a canonical language (usually English or Hindi for principles; vernacular for partner-specific playbooks). Every other language is translated — initially by machine, optionally line-edited by a human. Translation flags are reader-raised: "the translation of this page into Tamil is wrong."

Translation flags are per-language. The same page can have an open Tamil flag and a clean Kannada translation at the same time.

Where they go

To the translation queue, visible at /wiki/translation-queue. The city curator triages weekly; they assign to a human translator (us, or a partner staff member fluent in both languages) and the fixed translation publishes.

The page does not freeze while the translation is fixed

An open translation flag does not block edits to the canonical page. The canonical content moves forward; translations catch up. Readers in the affected language see a small banner: "translation has an open quality flag from MM/DD."

7.5The audit comparison — where flags come from systematically

Beyond reader-filed flags, two systematic flag sources exist.

  • Partner audit comparison. When a partner review meeting (Module 4) surfaces discrepancies between observed practice and documented playbook, those discrepancies are filed as flags — by the owner, on behalf of the partner. The partner gets credit; the flag carries the partner review ID.
  • Pitstop-runtime mismatch. When a pitstop checklist fails repeatedly across multiple settlements, the failure pattern is itself a flag — the practice as documented may be unrealistic. The ZL files these monthly during the zone review.

7.6Health metrics — the reader-channel dashboard

  • Open flags older than 7 days unacknowledged — should be zero.
  • Open flags older than 30 days unresolved — should be zero. Each one is a notification to the ZL.
  • Comment response time median — target ≤ 14 days.
  • Translation flag queue age — target median ≤ 14 days.
  • Decline rate on flags — target ≤ 10%. Higher means the page-owner is bunkering or the filers do not understand what a flag is for.

Check your understanding

Three questions — answer before you move on

  1. A CO files a flag saying "this page is wrong" without specifying section or reason. You are the owner. What do you do?
  2. A reader files a translation flag against the Tamil version of a page that just got a major canonical-content edit yesterday. What is the right next step, and what does the reader see in the meantime?
  3. An owner has six open flags older than 30 days. The curator has nudged twice. What is the third intervention, and who runs it?
