Module 1 · 45 minutes

The Three Page Types — Principle, Playbook, Runbook

All audiences Foundation · before anyone owns a page

A wiki with one shape of page becomes a swamp. We use exactly three. Each has a distinct purpose, a distinct cadence of updates, and a distinct kind of owner. Putting the wrong content in the wrong shape is the single most common mistake new owners make.

1.1The three shapes — at a glance

TypeAnswersUpdatesLives for
Principle Why we do what we do. The values that decide hard calls. Rarely. Only after deep reflection. Years.
Playbook How we do a specific kind of work — enrolment, monthly review, centre opening. Monthly-ish. Practice circles drive most edits. Many cohorts. Versioned.
Runbook The mechanical steps to navigate a specific scheme, system, or external process. Whenever the external rules shift — could be weekly. Until the scheme is replaced.

1.2Principle — the why

A principle is a single sentence we go back to when a decision is hard, a programme is drifting, or a partner is unsure what we will say next. "We exist to support the most vulnerable communities in this country" is a principle. So is "Frugality is alignment."

Principles are not values that get printed and forgotten. They are the things we return to when two needs compete and one has to win. They change rarely, and only after the leadership has sat with the change for a long time.

Who owns principles

Principles are owned at the leadership level. They are not surfaced to practice circles. They are not edited by COs. The review cadence is the same quarterly walkthrough as everything else — but for principles, "review" usually means "yes, this still holds." A principle that gets edited every quarter is no longer a principle.

For the working principles of how this team operates, see the wiki principle pages. They are read once, returned to often.

1.3Playbook — the how

A playbook captures how we do a specific kind of work — how we run a creche assessment, how we onboard a new food distribution point, how we close a quarterly partner review. The audience is the CO or RP about to do that work for the first time. They open the playbook, read for ten minutes, and start.

A good playbook

  • States its purpose in one sentence at the top.
  • Names the preconditions — when this playbook applies and what must be true before starting.
  • Walks the core flow as a numbered list of steps that a new joiner can execute.
  • Calls out the judgment calls — the places where the playbook bends, what to weigh.
  • Names the common failure modes — what goes wrong and how to spot it early.
  • Closes with "what good looks like" — concrete signals you got this right.
  • Links to related playbooks and runbooks.
A common mistake

New playbook authors try to capture every edge case. A playbook is not a legal document — it is a starting point. If 80% of the cases are handled, the playbook is done. The remaining 20% are judgment calls; the playbook names that they exist and points the reader toward the owner.

Who owns playbooks

Either a partner senior staff member or our RP, depending on whether the practice belongs to a single partner or is shared across partners in a domain. The owner is named. The owner term is 6 months. The owner runs the practice circle that feeds the playbook (during the first 3 months at least — Module 3 has the rules on rotation).

1.4Runbook — the mechanics

A runbook is the step-by-step for getting through a specific external process — a ration card application, an EWS certificate, an MSY pension enrolment, a scheme grievance escalation. The audience is the CO sitting with a citizen in a government office, phone in hand, trying to figure out what document is missing.

A good runbook

  • Names the scheme, the issuing authority, the typical office where you go.
  • Lists the documents required, with photographs of each if helpful.
  • Gives the step-by-step in order — what you fill, where you submit, what you collect.
  • States the realistic timeline — how long each step takes, total expected wait.
  • Lists the common rejection reasons and what to do about them.
  • Names the escalation path when the standard flow does not work.
  • Has a "Recent changes" section flagging scheme-rule shifts.
Runbooks rot the fastest

Scheme rules change without notice. A runbook that worked in January can mislead in April. Runbook owners watch for two signals: (a) a CO reports a step that no longer works, (b) the issuing office quietly changes a form. Both deserve a same-day edit to the "Recent changes" section, even if the full step-by-step has not been re-verified yet.

1.5Tagging — how readers find the page

Every page carries tags. The wiki dashboard filters by them. Get the tags right at creation and the page surfaces in the right searches forever; get them wrong and the page is invisible.

Tag typeExamplesUse when
verticalfood, water, sanitation, creche, elderly, youth, welfare-rightsAlways. Every page belongs to one vertical. Mandatory.
citybangalore, chennaiWhen the practice differs by city. Tag both if the practice is shared.
partner_orgsampark, cfar, swaayam, ...When the practice is specific to one partner's way of doing it.
activity_typemonthly-review, onboarding, closeout, ...When the page is process-shaped rather than domain-shaped.

If a page would deserve partner_org: sampark AND partner_org: cfar with different content under each, that is two pages, not one. Resist the urge to write the universal version — universals get diluted to nothing.

1.6Naming — the slug rule

Page titles are how humans find pages. Slugs are how the system addresses them. Get both right at creation; renaming is expensive once links exist.

  • Title: human-readable, what a CO would search for. "Creche monthly nutrition review" — not "Section 4.2 — Nutrition reporting protocol."
  • Slug: auto-derived, lowercase-kebab. Avoid renaming. If the title evolves but the slug is still meaningful, leave the slug.
  • Avoid acronyms in the title unless the acronym is what people actually say. "NRC" is fine; "Sectoral-CO" is not.
  • Avoid dates in titles. Versioning is automatic; the title should be timeless.

1.7Picking the right shape — the decision tree

When a gap is filed and an owner is about to start drafting, the first question is which of the three shapes. Use this in order.

  1. Is this answering "why do we do something this way?" → principle. Pause; principles are rarely the right answer for a new page. If yes, leadership review before drafting.
  2. Is this a step-by-step through a specific external process (a scheme, a system, a form)? → runbook.
  3. Is this how we run a specific kind of work — a recurring activity, an event, an onboarding? → playbook.
  4. Could it be split into one playbook + one runbook? → split. Two clearer pages beat one ambiguous page.
Check your understanding

Three questions — answer before you move on

  1. "How we decide which settlement gets a new creche" — principle, playbook, or runbook? Why?
  2. The Government of Karnataka changes the documents required for the Anganwadi Helper appointment overnight. Which page type is affected and how fast should it be edited?
  3. You inherit a 15-page principle document covering how the team approaches partner relationships. What do you do with it?
