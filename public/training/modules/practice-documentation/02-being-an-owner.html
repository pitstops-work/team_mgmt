Module 2 · 75 minutes

Being an Owner — Term, Accountability, Drafting

Owner RP Mandatory before accepting a first term

Owning a wiki page is a small commitment with a sharp edge. Most days you do nothing; on the days you act, the action is consequential — a draft published, a practice circle convened, a handover completed. This module is the operating manual for a 6-month term.

2.1What you are accountable for

Owning a page means you are accountable for five things. Not four, not six. The list is short on purpose.

  1. The page is current. If a CO walks the practice today, the page reflects what they should do.
  2. The practice circle happens. On the date it said it would. Even after facilitation rotates to someone else.
  3. Flags and comments are acknowledged within seven days, resolved within thirty. Not delegated, not deferred.
  4. The 90-day review is on your calendar and gets done. Walk the page; close the cycle.
  5. The handover at month six is real. Listen-tour, draft notes, conversations with attendees, not a name swap on the metadata.
What you are not accountable for

You are not the police of the practice. You are not the trainer of every CO. You are not responsible for translation quality (the translation flag queue handles that). You are not the curator (cross-page consistency is a separate role). And you are not solo — you have a circle behind you.

2.2The 6-month term — what the clock looks like

Every owner term is 6 months. The calendar is the same regardless of whether the page is brand new or in its eighth term.

The "you facilitate for the first 3 circles" rule reflects a deliberate choice: a new owner needs the room before delegating it. After month 3, facilitation rotates among the CO attendees. You stay in the room as a participant and scribe.

2.3The drafting protocol — 48 hours from trigger to draft

A page edit is always triggered by an upstream event — a circle, a partner review, a shadow visit, a gap, a flag, a quarterly review. Within 48 hours of the trigger, the edit is in draft. Not necessarily published — but visible, with the linked trigger, so the people who were in the room can see what you took away.

The 48-hour rule

Past 48 hours, momentum is gone. The attendees have moved on. The case is no longer fresh. You will draft something thinner, less accurate, less honest. If you cannot draft within 48 hours, the circle was wasted — and that is a signal that needs surfacing, not hidden.

A draft is not a publish

The wiki distinguishes draft from published deliberately. Drafts circulate to the people who were in the room (circle attendees, partner review attendees) for two business days. They line-edit, push back, suggest sharpenings. Only then do you publish. The version note records both the trigger and the people who reviewed.

  • Trigger — every draft links back to its source. Circle ID, partner review ID, flag ID, gap ID.
  • Change note — one line. What changed and why. Future owners will read this line a hundred times more than the surrounding paragraphs.
  • Reviewer list — who saw the draft before publish. Even informally — three names in a WhatsApp thread is fine.
  • Translation flag — when you publish, the translation pipeline kicks off automatically. Do not wait for translations; publish in English (or the canonical language) and let translators catch up.

2.4The quarterly review — what "walking the page" means

Every 90 days you walk the page, even if nothing has changed. The walk is not a ceremony — it is five concrete questions, in order:

  1. Is the opening sentence still true? Sometimes the practice the page describes has quietly died.
  2. Does the "core flow" match what a good CO actually does today? Walk one yourself in your head, step by step.
  3. Have the "common failure modes" shifted? Add the ones we have seen this quarter; demote the ones that no longer happen.
  4. Are the related-pages links still right? Pages get retired; links rot.
  5. Are there flags, comments, or gaps that should now be closed? A quarterly review is when you sweep the queue.
A clean review still counts

If the page is current and nothing needs to change, record the review with a one-line note: "No changes; current as of MM/YYYY." The point is to know the page was looked at, not to force an edit.

2.5The handover — six things, in this order

Owner terms are 6 months because fresh eyes are non-negotiable. Handover is the discipline that makes that real. Even if you intend to renew, the steps below are mandatory.

  • Six weeks before term-end, leadership is told whether you intend to renew. Quiet signal, no drama.
  • If not renewing, you propose a successor — by name. Leadership can override. You do not just leave the page empty.
  • You write a one-page handover note. Three sections: what shifted this term, what is still open, what I would not change.
  • You walk the proposed successor through the page in one sitting. In person. Not over chat.
  • The successor accepts in the system. The page metadata flips; their 6-month clock starts.
  • You attend the next circle as a regular participant. No reassertion of "I used to own this."
Stagger your principle pages

If you own multiple principle pages, do not let all their terms expire on the same day. Manually stagger term-ends across the calendar. Otherwise renewal becomes a rubber-stamp batch — which defeats the whole point of a term limit.

2.6The owner's anti-patterns

Four ways a 6-month term goes wrong. Watch for them in yourself.

Anti-patternSmellCorrection
The silent term No circles run. No flags triaged. Page version count: 0. You are not owning. Hand back to leadership now, not in three months.
The owner monologue Circle minutes are mostly the owner explaining the playbook to COs. Switch facilitation to a CO immediately, even before month 3 if needed.
The aspiration drift Edits describe how the practice should work, not how it does. Shadow visit. Walk one with a real CO. Rewrite to match.
The bunker You start declining flags and comments without consulting the circle. Bring the disputed flag to the next circle as the case. The room decides.
Check your understanding

Four questions — answer before you move on

  1. You facilitated three circles. It is month 4. A CO is supposed to facilitate but cancels the night before. What do you do?
  2. You publish a page edit triggered by a partner review. What three pieces of metadata must be on the version note before you click publish?
  3. Two weeks before your term ends, your proposed successor declines. The page will have no owner in 14 days. What is your sequence?
  4. Halfway through your term, a CO raises a flag that the playbook contradicts what every CO at her partner actually does. Whose side is the wiki on, and what do you do this week?
