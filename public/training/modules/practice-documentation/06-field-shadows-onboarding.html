Module 6 · 90 minutes

Field Shadows, New-CO Onboarding & the Surprise Log

Owner RP CO The observation channels · easy to skip, hard to recover

A practice circle catches what people remember. A partner review catches what leadership decided. Field shadows catch what is actually happening — drift, workarounds, things nobody thought were worth mentioning. New-CO onboarding catches what is missing — every "I don't get X" question from someone new is a documentation gap in waiting. The surprise log catches what would otherwise be forgotten in three days. Three small processes; together they double the wiki's signal.

6.1Field shadow visits — the owner walks alongside

A field shadow is the owner (or another CO) walking alongside a CO doing a documented activity. They observe; they do not coach. They take notes against the playbook. Single biggest source of drift detection.

Cadence

  • Owner: at least one shadow visit per month across the partners covered by the page.
  • RP: one shadow visit per partner per quarter, regardless of page ownership.
  • A CO can shadow another CO from a different partner — once per quarter, by mutual agreement. Cross-partner shadowing.

The shadow protocol

  • Tell the CO you are shadowing, not auditing. Same words: "I am here to watch how the practice actually runs. I am taking notes against the playbook, not against you."
  • Walk the full activity end to end. Do not leave halfway — half-shadows produce half-truths.
  • Note three columns: what the playbook says, what the CO actually did, why the difference (if asked).
  • Do not correct in the moment. The point is to learn the practice; correcting changes what you see.
  • Debrief after, in 15 minutes. Ask the CO what was hardest, what was unclear, what the playbook missed.
The page is wrong, not the CO

If you find a difference between the playbook and what a good CO does, the playbook is the suspect. A CO who consistently deviates from a playbook in the same direction has usually figured out something the playbook missed. Edit the playbook; do not retrain the CO. (Exception: dignity / safety violations are corrected on the spot regardless.)

From shadow visit to page edit

Same 48-hour SLA. The shadow visit produces a draft edit or a gap entry within 48 hours. If no edit and no gap, the visit was social, not a shadow.

6.2New-CO onboarding sessions

Every new CO joining a partner is walked through the relevant playbooks by the owner. Not by a peer CO. Not by a handed-down notebook. By the owner of the wiki page that governs the practice.

This costs the owner two hours per new CO. It is the single best two hours an owner ever spends — for the owner.

Why the owner runs onboarding

  • Fresh eyes. A new CO has not learned to ignore what is unclear. They will ask the questions a veteran has stopped asking.
  • Free user research. Every "I don't get this" is a documentation-gap signal the owner cannot ignore.
  • Relationship. The new CO knows from day one who to call when a page is wrong. The flag/gap channels stay open.
  • Quality. Onboarding the new CO well is the cheapest way to keep the practice at standard. A bad onboarding takes 6 months to undo.

The session protocol

  • Schedule within the new CO's first two weeks. Not first day (too much) and not month two (already learned bad habits).
  • Walk through the playbook in the order a real activity unfolds. Not in section order — in chronological order.
  • The new CO is encouraged to interrupt. "What does that mean?" is the right behaviour, not a sign of weakness.
  • Every question they ask is logged. Even small ones. The log becomes the gap-queue input.
  • Close with: "Of everything I said today, what would you change in the page if you could?" Capture the answer literally.
Capture the questions, do not answer all of them

The temptation is to answer every question elegantly. Resist. Some questions deserve a page edit, not a verbal answer. Log them; let the next circle synthesise; let the page improve. The new CO benefits more from a wiki that incorporates their question than from your elegant answer in the moment.

6.3The surprise log — capturing what would otherwise be lost

A surprise is something that happened in the field this week that you did not expect. A breakdown, a workaround, an outcome that surprised you, a household reaction you had not seen before. A surprise log is a one-line capture of these — filed quickly, triaged weekly, fed into the next practice circle.

Without a surprise log, the field's most interesting signals get forgotten in three days. With it, they accumulate into the case shortlist for every practice circle.

How the surprise log works

  • Channel — a WhatsApp group, an inbox, or a "surprise" link on the wiki. Whichever the team uses; not all three.
  • Format — one line. Date, vertical, settlement (or partner), one-sentence surprise. No analysis.
  • Frequency — whenever it happens. Most COs file 1–3 per month; that is the right rate.
  • Triage — owner reads the queue once a week. Promotes interesting items into the next circle's case shortlist; closes the rest.
The surprise that is actually a flag

If the surprise contradicts a playbook, it is a flag — file it as one. The surprise log is for things that no page covers and that the field has not yet named. Be honest about the distinction; the queues stay clean.

6.4Cross-partner exchange — once a quarter

Once a quarter, a CO from partner A spends one day with partner B's domain team. Same vertical, different organisation. The CO observes, asks questions, and writes a half-page note for the next practice circle.

This is the cheapest way to catch myths — the "everyone does X this way" beliefs that turn out to be specific to one partner.

  • Owner coordinates the exchange. Pairs are agreed at the start of the quarter.
  • The visiting CO writes a half-page note within 48 hours. Same SLA.
  • The note goes into the next circle's pre-read.
  • Pages that need editing are flagged from the note.

6.5Goal-closeout reviews — feeding the circle

When a goal cycle ends on the Pitstops app — a programme cohort closes, a campaign wraps — there is a 30-minute closeout in the field. "What would we tell the next team doing this." The notes from this conversation feed the next practice circle directly.

This is not a separate forum with new attendees. It is a discipline at the end of every goal cycle: spend half an hour, write the takeaways, file the gaps, and let the next circle synthesise.

6.6How the four feeds compose

Shadow visit, onboarding session, surprise log, cross-partner exchange, goal closeout — five small upstream channels. Each is light by itself; together they create the case stream for practice circles, which create the page edits, which keep the wiki current. None of them is optional; none of them is heavy.

If your circle pre-read is thin

The most reliable diagnostic of an under-fed circle is "we don't have good cases for next month." That is never a circle problem — it is always an upstream problem. One of the four feeds has gone quiet. Find which, restart it.

Check your understanding

Four questions — answer before you move on

  1. You shadow a CO who skips a step in the playbook. The CO explains the skip with a sensible reason. What three things go into your notebook before you leave?
  2. You are running a new CO onboarding. The CO asks five questions you cannot answer cleanly because the page is silent on each. What do you do at the end of the session?
  3. The surprise log has produced 14 entries this month, all from one CO. The other six COs in the domain have filed zero. What is the most likely diagnosis?
  4. Your circle pre-reads have been thin for two months. Walk through the five upstream feeds and name where you would start your diagnosis.
