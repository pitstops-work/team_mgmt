Module 8 · 75 minutes

Quarterly Reviews & the 6-Month Handover

Owner ZL Curator The long-cycle disciplines · most-skipped, most-load-bearing

The processes earlier in this curriculum operate on monthly and weekly rhythms. This module covers the two slower rhythms: the 90-day owner review, and the 180-day owner handover. They are the easiest disciplines to skip because nothing acute happens when you skip them — until six months later, when the page is silently wrong.

8.1The 90-day review — what "walking the page" means

Every 90 days, the owner walks the page. Even if nothing has changed. Even if no flags arrived. The walk is not a ceremony; it is five concrete questions in order — the same five from Module 2, repeated here because they matter.

  1. Is the opening sentence still true?
  2. Does the core flow match what a good CO actually does today?
  3. Have the common failure modes shifted?
  4. Are the related-pages links still right?
  5. Are there flags, comments, gaps that should now be closed?
Recording a clean review

If nothing needed to change, you still record the review with a one-line note: "No changes; current as of MM/YYYY." The point is provable freshness, not forced edits. A page with five consecutive "no changes" review records is a healthy mature page, not a dead one — provided the comment thread and flag queue are also alive.

8.2Cadence by page type

The 90-day default applies to playbooks and runbooks. Principles get a different rhythm, and runbooks may need faster review if external rules shift.

Page typeDefault review cadenceModifier
Playbook90 daysIf domain is high-velocity (food, water), review may piggyback on monthly circles — no separate quarterly walk needed.
Runbook90 daysFaster if the external scheme changed. Trigger a same-day "Recent changes" edit; full review within 2 weeks.
Principle90 days, batchedOwner walks all owned principles in one sitting once per quarter. 90 minutes for 12 principles is right.

Batching principle reviews

If you own multiple principle pages — for example, all twelve working principles — review them together once a quarter. Sit with them as a set. Ask: are these still our principles? Has anything we have done this quarter contradicted any of them? Has any of them stopped being load-bearing?

Most quarters the answer is "no changes." Record it that way. The discipline of asking the question is what matters.

The principle that gets edited every quarter is no longer a principle

If a principle page has had edits in three consecutive quarterly reviews, the page may not be a principle — it may be a playbook in disguise. The next handover conversation is a good place to ask whether the page should be restructured.

8.3The stale-page sweep — when the owner misses a review

The system tracks lastReviewedAt on every page. The curator runs an automated stale-page sweep weekly.

Page age since last reviewWhat happens
0–90 daysGREEN. Normal.
90–120 daysAMBER. Owner gets a nudge. Curator notes on the dashboard.
120–180 daysRED. Owner gets a second nudge. ZL is copied.
> 180 daysPage marked orphaned. Visible to readers. Curator finds a new owner; existing owner term ends early.

An orphaned page is not retired — it is just visibly without an active owner. Readers see the orphan banner; they know to treat the page with extra caution until a new owner is named.

8.4The 6-month handover — six steps in order

The handover protocol from Module 2, restated and expanded. Even if you intend to renew, the steps are mandatory.

Step 1 · Renewal signal — 6 weeks before

You tell leadership privately whether you intend to renew for another term. No drama, no committee. A one-line signal: "I'd like to renew" or "I won't be renewing."

Step 2 · Successor proposal — 4 weeks before

If not renewing, you propose a successor by name. Leadership can override but rarely does. If renewing, you skip this step.

Step 3 · Handover note — 3 weeks before

You write a one-page handover note. Three sections:

  • What shifted this term. The 3–5 most consequential edits to the page, with the why.
  • What is still open. Live flags, unfinished gaps, conversations not yet resolved.
  • What I would not change. Things that look ripe for change but you have learned should not be touched. Most valuable section.

Step 4 · Listen-tour with the successor — 2 weeks before

You walk the successor through the page in one sitting. In person. Not over chat. They take their own notes; you do not hand them yours. They ask; you answer.

Separately, the successor is encouraged to do their own listen-tour: one conversation with two COs, one with the curator, one with a peer owner of an adjacent page. This is not optional — it is how the successor starts their term with the room, not on top of the room.

Step 5 · Acceptance — handover day

The successor accepts in the system. The page ownerId flips; ownerTermStart resets to today; ownerTermEnd = today + 6 months. Your handover note attaches to the page version history.

Step 6 · Stepping back — the first month after

You attend the next circle as a regular participant. You do not reassert "I used to own this." You do not jump in when a flag arrives — let the new owner handle it. If you see something going wrong, you raise it privately to them, not in the room.

Hand back, do not hand off mid-term

If you realise mid-term you cannot do this work — busy season, changed role, lost interest — hand back to leadership immediately. Do not let the page degrade for three months waiting for the natural term-end. The next owner needs the page in a workable state, not a state needing recovery.

8.5Staggering principle terms — the cliff problem

If a single owner holds many principle pages, all their terms expire on the same day. That is a cliff. Even if the owner re-ups, the renewal becomes a rubber-stamp batch — which defeats the term-limit's purpose of forcing fresh attention.

The fix is mechanical and one-time: at first creation, stagger the term-end dates across the calendar. Twelve principles spread across six months means one renewal every two weeks rather than twelve on one day.

  • Same approach for any owner who finds themselves holding more than three pages of the same type.
  • The curator runs the stagger calculation at page-creation time; the owner does not have to think about it.
  • After the first cycle, terms naturally stay staggered.

8.6The post-handover surface — what new owners should not have to figure out

The new owner walks in. The page exists, the handover note exists, the listen-tour happened. What else makes their first month easier?

  • A list of open flags, gaps, comments, with status and age.
  • The previous owner's review notes from the last 90-day walk.
  • The last three circle minutes for this page's domain.
  • The last partner review meeting record for partners this page covers.
  • A note from the curator on cross-page consistency issues affecting this page.
  • Translation status by language, with any open flags.

All of this is generated automatically from the wiki state at handover. The new owner does not have to ask for it; the dashboard surfaces it.

Check your understanding

Four questions — answer before you move on

  1. It is your 90-day review day. The page has had no flags, no comments, no shadow-visit notes since the last review. You walk it; nothing needs to change. What do you record?
  2. Your principle page has been edited in three consecutive quarterly reviews. What conversation does that trigger, with whom?
  3. Two weeks before your term ends, your proposed successor accepts and then withdraws after one shadow visit. The clock is ticking. What is your sequence?
  4. You walk into a page you are taking over. The dashboard shows 12 open flags, 4 stale comments, and a translation flag in Tamil. Where do you start?
