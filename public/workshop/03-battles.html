THE GUILLOTINE

"Rights-based" vs "service-delivery"

A binary the sector has been arguing about for forty years. Both sides are partly right. The argument has produced almost nothing.

Rights-based camp
"Don't fix the symptom. Fight for the entitlement."
  • Policy advocacy, legal aid, public-interest litigation
  • "The State has the duty — we hold it accountable"
  • Strong moral language. Strong PMU presence. Strong donor narrative.
  • Programmes are slow. Outputs are intermediate. Vulnerability stays.
VS
Service-delivery camp
"Don't wait for the State. Deliver what's needed."
  • Schools, clinics, kitchens, hostels — run by NGOs
  • "Something is better than nothing — meanwhile"
  • Visible outputs. Easy donor reporting. Vulnerable to looking like substitution.
  • Programmes are fast. Sustainability is a constant question. Scale is rare.

For decades, each camp has claimed higher moral ground over the other. Conferences run on this fight. Careers run on this fight. The vulnerable households watch us argue and wait.

PMUs IN SECRETARIATS · "ADVOCACY WORK"

A specific failure mode of the rights-based wing.

The professionalised advocacy industry. Well-funded. Well-spoken. Well-distant from the household.

A "Project Management Unit" inside a state secretariat — funded by a foundation, staffed by consultants — drafting policy briefs the state may or may not adopt. The PMU exists for three years. A handful of briefs get filed. The settlements outside the secretariat building are unchanged.
"Advocacy work" as a category has become a place to park budgets that don't have to show outcomes. There is always another consultation, another roundtable, another submission. Nothing in a community needs to actually change.
And the moral framing is intact: "We are working on the systemic root cause." It is impossible to disprove. It is also impossible to evaluate. That's the appeal.
WHERE WE STAND

We walk cleanly away from both sides.

Not because we are above it. Because the choice itself is rigged.

Our work is a single, simple act: we identify a need in a community and we try to address it in the best way available to us. That's it. That's the whole stance.

If "addressing the need" needs us to demand from the State
— we will. Quietly. Concretely. For a specific household. Not as a campaign. Not from a stage.
If "addressing the need" needs us to deliver a service ourselves
— we will. Carefully. Frugally. While being honest that this is substitution, and substitution is fragile.

Neither rights-based nor service-delivery. Both, when the situation calls for them. Neither, when it doesn't. The community decides, not the donor camp.

MYTH ONE

"Sustainability" as the sector uses the word.

A word that has done more damage to good programmes than almost any other.

Farce

Donors ask: "How will this be sustainable when our funding ends?"

The honest answer, ninety percent of the time, is: "It won't. It will collapse, mostly, within six months of funding withdrawal."

Crèches close. Health workers leave. Community groups stop meeting. Records stop being kept. The structures only existed because a programme paid for them.

But the sector cannot say this — because donors won't fund work that admits this — so the sector invents a "sustainability plan": community contribution targets nobody believes, government linkage promises nobody secured, social enterprise revenue projections nobody will hit.

It is a ritual. Everyone in the room knows it is a ritual. Nobody calls it out.

WHAT WE SAY INSTEAD

"This programme will work as long as we run it well. After that, it's anyone's guess."

An uncomfortable sentence. Also a true one. We say it to donors, to partners, to ourselves.

It does not mean we don't try for durability
We absolutely do. We invest in community leaders. We push for State linkage. We build records the partner can use. But we don't pretend the structure survives funding withdrawal. Honesty is the first step.
It does mean we stop pretending fragile things are robust
A meal programme is fragile. A community group is fragile. A creche is fragile. Calling them "sustainable" because we wrote a sustainability plan does not make them so. Naming the fragility is what lets us protect them.
MYTH TWO

"Community participation"

The other word that has been hollowed out by decades of misuse.

Farce

Every project document says it. Every donor demands it. Every report claims it happened.

In practice, most "community participation" is: five people from a settlement signing a register at a meeting they were paid ₹100 to attend, at which an NGO described a programme it had already designed.

When the NGO leaves three years later, the "participating community" has no memory of having decided anything. Because they didn't decide. They attended.

Real participation — the community shaping what gets done, when, how, with what trade-offs — is rare. It is also messy, slow, and produces things donors didn't ask for. Which is why most NGOs simulate it instead.

HOW WE TRY TO DO IT HONESTLY

Participation is what the community can vote down — not just what they attended.

We walk the settlement before we design. Not a focus group. Not a survey. We sit, we ask, we listen, we change our minds.
We work with community leaders who already exist — even the awkward ones — not committees we invented for the project.
We let the community say no. If a settlement doesn't want a creche, we don't open one there. We don't try harder. We move to a settlement that does.
We don't claim "100% community-led". We will admit, in the report, that we designed it and the community accepted it. That's still better than most.
THE BIG STRUCTURAL TRUTH

The social sector mostly works for its own survival.

Not because the people are bad. Because the incentives are.

NGO needs grants to survive. Grants come from donors. Donors prefer certain narratives (sustainability, scale, replicability, systems change). NGOs adapt their language and programmes to match. The community's actual need slowly stops being the centre of gravity.
Over enough years, the sector starts to perform virtue rather than do work. There is a moral self-image — that we are the good ones, that we are on the right side, that we are above the corporate world or the State. This becomes a kind of secular godliness. It is intoxicating and very hard to walk away from.
When we critique another NGO, we are usually told "don't air dirty laundry, we are all on the same side." That phrase has protected an enormous amount of bad work over the decades.
We are inside this sector. We carry these incentives too. Pretending we are immune is the surest way to get captured.
OUR ANSWER

Confront. Politely. Persistently. Mostly with ourselves first.

If we keep calling a farce a farce — slowly, things shift.

When we hear "sustainability" — ask: "What specifically will still be running 12 months after funding ends, and who will pay for it?"
When we hear "community participation" — ask: "Did the community shape this, or did they just attend?"
When we hear "scale" or "replicate" — ask: "How well has it worked in the second settlement? The third? Or are we replicating something that has only run once?"
When we hear "systems change" — ask: "Which specific household, in which specific settlement, will feel this in the next six months?"
When we are the ones saying these words — pause longer. The hardest critique to run is on the things we ourselves are tempted to say.

Common sense and conscience are stronger filters than any framework. Trust them. Especially when a framework wants you to override them.

QUESTIONS

This is the slot to disagree.

If you have run rights-based work and feel insulted — say so. If you have run service work and feel slighted — say so. The point isn't comfort. The point is clarity.

"Aren't we being smug by 'walking away from both sides'? Isn't that its own moral high ground?"
"What about the partners we work with who do call themselves rights-based?"
"If we admit something isn't sustainable, won't donors refuse to fund it?"
"Some of our reports use words like 'community-led' too. Are we being hypocritical?"
"This whole framing assumes we know better. What if we don't?"
