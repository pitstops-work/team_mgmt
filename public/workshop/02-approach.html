FIRST PRINCIPLE

We are deeply skeptical of "models". We are deeply open to learning.

These two go together. They are not in tension.

The sector loves the word "model". The Pratham model. The SEWA model. The Gram Vikas model. Models are how the sector packages success for fundraising. They are almost always retrofits — an honest mess that got cleaned up for a deck.
We borrow from anyone who is doing something we admire. We do not adopt their model. We adopt their specific working practice, and we keep testing it ourselves before we believe it.
If someone presents you a model — ask what they had to throw away to make it presentable. That's where the truth lives.
HOW WE LEARN

Try and learn. Not theorize and predict.

Pick a small thing. Run it. Watch what breaks. Fix that. Move.

What we do
Start small. One settlement. One pitstop. One cycle. See it through. Document what broke. Bring the next cycle's plan to a review. Adjust. Run again.
What we don't do
Write a 40-page strategy. Run a stakeholder workshop. Pre-design "the right model". Wait for "evidence-based best practice". By the time you've done that, six cohorts have come and gone.

A failed cycle that taught us something is more valuable than three planning offsites that decided nothing.

THE NON-NEGOTIABLE

Rigour. Every day. Not once a quarter.

This is the single hardest thing about working here.

Rigour means →
A pitstop has a date. The date is held. If the date slips, there is a reason in writing and a new date. Not "we'll get to it."
Rigour means →
A checklist item is either Done or it is not. "Mostly done" is not a status. If you say it's done, someone can walk in tomorrow and see it.
Rigour means →
A number on a report has a source. The source can be traced. If we can't trace it, we don't put it in the report.
Rigour means →
A visit happens on the date it was promised. To the partner. To the settlement. To the team member. Calendars are not aspirational.
WHERE WE WORK

Where the need is. Not where the panel is.

This decides almost every choice we make.

We choose
A settlement with no NGO presence over a settlement that already has three. A creche that runs in a 8×8 ft room over a flagship centre. The household nobody is counting over the one in every survey.
We deliberately decline
Glamour projects. Photo-op settlements. "Demonstration sites" that everyone tours but no one lives in. Programmes designed to be replicable before they have been done once.

When we have to choose between visible and necessary — we choose necessary. The visible work will find its own funding. The necessary work usually won't.

HOW WE DELIVER

Four ways. One spectrum.

Each of these is appropriate in some contexts and disastrous in others. Knowing which to use is most of the craft.

1
Direct
Our staff. Our methods. Our presence in the settlement every day. Highest control, highest cost, hardest to scale — and the only way to truly learn a programme before we ask others to do it.
2
Indirect, fully owned
A partner runs the day-to-day. We design the programme, set the cadence, write the rulebook, sit in every review. The accountability is ours. We don't hide behind the partner.
3
Regular grants (aligned partners)
A partner runs their programme — their design, their methods. We support because they're doing what we would have wanted to do. We hold rigour on outcomes, not on method.
4
Operational grants
In between #2 and #3. Sometimes leaning toward co-design, sometimes toward trust. The point is honesty: name where on the spectrum each grant sits, and don't pretend otherwise.

A mistake we keep watching for: treating a #3 partner like a #2, or a #2 partner like a #3. Both produce resentment and bad work. Be honest about which one this is.

HOW WE ACTUALLY WORK

Field-mostly. Desk for the desk-able.

A simple split that most NGOs get wrong by inverting.

On the field (≈ 70–80% of an RP / ZL week)
Settlement visits. Household conversations. Pitstop reviews on site. Partner check-ins. Co-walking with community leaders. Watching the work happen — not hearing about it.
At the desk (≈ 20–30%)
Pitstops app updates. Document uploads. Voice notes. Practice notes. Activity logging. Reading the weekly digest. Drafting the next month's plan.
All the AI we have built is to make desk work cheap.
Voice → transcript. AI-supported document upload → checklist done. Draft notes → polished. Calendar → scheduled. Use it. The less time you spend on desk, the more time you spend on the field — that's the whole point.
TRAVEL AND COST CULTURE

Two-wheeler. Auto. Cab only when it makes sense.

Not a rule. A signal. Of what kind of organisation we want to be.

🛵 Default: two-wheeler
Settlement visits. Cluster runs. Most days, most distances. Cheap, fast, parks anywhere, and signals we are not "above" the work.
🛺 Otherwise: auto
If you can't take a two-wheeler — distance, equipment, weather. Auto is the next step. Not an Ola. Not an Uber. Local auto.
🚕 Cab — when it makes sense
Long inter-city. Heavy/sensitive material. Late-night staff transport. With a partner who needs to be picked up. Visiting more than two settlements in a day with no auto network. Use judgement, then mention it in the travel request.

Whatever is the simplest way to get the work done in the most frugal way for that specific situation — without sacrificing safety and convenience.

SIGNAL TO TEAM AND PARTNERS

Cost culture is contagious. So is its absence.

How you spend tells everyone around you what is normal.

If a senior person flies business and stays in a 5-star, every partner staff member starts asking why their per-diem is ₹450.
If we treat a ₹50,000 catering budget for an internal review as nothing — partners will assume we have no idea what ₹50,000 means in their settlement.
If our office is plush, our equipment expensive, our travel luxurious — the entire moral case for "every rupee counts" collapses. We become like everyone we criticise.
Frugality is not poverty cosplay. It is alignment. Unit costs matter — not total budget value.
WHAT WE DON'T DO

No credit. No branding. No stage.

Three things we have deliberately chosen to stay away from. Forever.

We do not take credit for partner work
If a partner ran the children centre, the partner ran the children centre. We helped. That's all. Our name does not need to be on the banner, the report cover, the impact deck, or the LinkedIn post.
We do not brand
No T-shirts with our logo at community events. No branded hoardings in settlements. No "powered by" footers. The community does not need to know our name.
We do not go on stage
No conferences. No policy panels. No public speeches. When approached — we decline politely and get back to work. Our time goes to the field, not the audience.

The reward for good work is more good work — not visibility. The day visibility becomes the goal, we have stopped being who we are.

QUESTIONS

Push on this too.

20–25 minutes. Get the disagreements on the table now, not over WhatsApp at 11pm.

"How do we know we are inside our approach and not slowly drifting into someone else's?"
"What if a partner wants to put our name on something — out of respect, not branding?"
"Is 'no conferences' absolute? What about technical workshops where we'd actually learn?"
"Where's the line on cab use? I've seen staff stranded at 9pm in an unsafe area."
"Type 4 grants — operational. How do we know which side of the spectrum a specific grant sits on?"
