Partners and team

How we hold rigour with the people we work with — without becoming the donor-on-the-head we ourselves complain about.

RULE ONE

Rigour — non-negotiable. Surprise visits — only when necessary.

These sound like they contradict. They don't, if you set it up right.

Rigour means scheduled visits happen
Every month, the date is held. The partner knows we are coming. The settlement knows the day. The pitstop has its review. We are predictable — and we are present.
Surprise visits — sparingly
When something feels off. When a report contradicts what we saw last month. When a partner asks for them. Not as a default culture of suspicion. Suspicion-as-default destroys partnerships.

A partner who knows you visit on the 5th every month, and who knows you also have the right to visit on the 17th if something looks wrong, is more honest than one who lives in constant ambush.

THE DONOR NARRATIVE

"Donors sit on partners' heads. Donors dictate programmes."

A common critique. It is true. It is also false. Both at the same time.

The "true" part
Donor reporting demands have ballooned. Donor pet themes shape what partners apply for. Donor fragility has produced partners that perform compliance more than work. Decades of grant history have skewed the social sector — that is real.
The "false" part
The critique often hides a deeper truth: many CSOs like having the donor decide. It outsources accountability. "We had to do it — the donor wanted it" is easier than "We chose this, and we own it."
The deeper bug
The whole donor-partner-community chain rests on an overestimation of what a CSO actually is. Strip the language — and a CSO is a small intermediary organisation, with limited capacity, executing programmes for funders, in communities that did not elect them.
THE UNCOMFORTABLE LINE

CSOs are middlemen. CBOs are middlemen. We are middlemen.

Once you say this out loud, a lot of other things become easier to see.

A "Community-Based Organisation" is, almost always, a small set of self-appointed representatives. Sometimes elected, often not. Sometimes accountable to the community, often not. They are intermediaries — just like we are. They have their own incentives, their own politics, their own survival anxieties.

The "voice of the community" — when delivered through a CBO or a CSO — has already passed through at least one filter. We need to know whose filter.

This is not a reason to stop working with CBOs and partners. It is a reason to stop romanticising them, and to keep going back to the community ourselves. The household is the ground truth. Everything else is intermediation.

WHAT WE OWE THE PARTNER

Politeness. Equality. Empathy. As a baseline, not a bonus.

If our team treats a partner staff member as junior, that's a failure of culture — not a personality quirk.

Equal seating. Same chair, same tea, same address. Not "sir / madam" from the partner and first names from us.
No talking down. Even when we are sure they are wrong. The way to win that argument is to ask better questions, not to lecture.
Honour their constraints. A partner staff member has rent to pay, children at home, a phone bill, a relative in hospital. We are not the centre of their universe. Plan visits around that, not against it.
Take their criticism seriously. A partner is often the first to spot a flaw in our design. If we hear "yes sir" from a partner all the time — we are doing something wrong.
BUT — NOT NAIVE

Politeness is not the same as trust. Integrity is not assumed — it is verified.

The sector has real integrity problems. Pretending it doesn't is its own form of dishonesty.

Ghost beneficiaries. Inflated headcounts. Vendor kickbacks. Salary leakage. Inflated travel. Bills that don't add up. All of it exists in the sector. We have seen all of it.
Politeness gives the partner the dignity. The verification gives the work integrity. Both at the same time. Pick one — and you've broken something.
If a number on a report can't be traced, we ask — politely — to trace it. If it still can't be traced, we don't pay against it. Quietly. Firmly. Without making a scene.
UNIT COSTS · FRUGALITY

Never forget what one rupee costs in a settlement.

A discipline we carry into every spending decision — ours, the partner's, the donor's.

A meal is ₹36. A training session costs ₹500 per person per day. A creche teacher's monthly salary is ₹12,000. A community group meeting cost is ₹1,500. These are the units. Hold them in your head while you read every budget line.
A ₹2 lakh venue cost for an event = ~5,500 meals. A ₹3 lakh consultant fee = roughly two years of a children-centre teacher. Express budgets in what they replace, not just what they cost.
We and the partner — both should know the monthly burn rate for the next month, and planning should happen accordingly. Expenditure cannot be a hockey stick.
HISTORY · JUDGEMENT

We will be judged. We are ready.

Every confident programme of the last fifty years has eventually faced its reckoning. Ours will too.

1960s · GREEN REVOLUTION
"We solved hunger."
Later judged: aquifer depletion, soil degradation, suicide in farming families, monoculture, dependency on input chains. The intent was real. The damage was also real.
1970s · WHITE REVOLUTION
"We made India self-sufficient in milk."
Now visible: the production-led narrative was overstated; cooperative structures captured by middlemen; nutrition outcomes uneven. Some of the foundational claims are now read as half-truths at best.
2000s+ · LARGE NGO/CSR PROGRAMMES
"AHADs and similar — celebrated, replicated, judged."
Internal critiques and external evaluations now read very differently from the early triumphalism. Several flagship programmes are being quietly revisited. The model stories were not the whole stories.
202X · US
Whatever we do confidently today will be re-read in twenty years.
Our creche model. Our welfare-rights playbook. Our way of thinking about partners. Some will hold up. Some will be embarrassing. We don't yet know which is which. We accept that.

What we can do today: stay grounded, listen to the field, follow common sense and conscience, and not perform certainty we don't have. History can judge us later. The middlemen circus does not get to judge us now.

QUESTIONS

Short slot for this one — but make it count.

"Calling our partners — and ourselves — 'middlemen' feels harsh. Is there a better word that's still honest?"
"How do we run a verification without making the partner feel suspected?"
"What does 'integrity verified' look like in practice — what's the checklist?"
"When does a surprise visit actually become necessary?"
"What's the right way to respond to a pushback from a partner?"
