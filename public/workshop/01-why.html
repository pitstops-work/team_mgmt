THE OBJECTIVE

Support the most vulnerable communities in this country.

That's the whole sentence. No qualifiers. No clauses about "systems change" or "scale."

If you ever feel lost about what we are doing — come back to this line. Most decisions in our work resolve themselves the moment you put this sentence at the top.

"Most vulnerable" is not a slogan. It's an operational filter. When two needs compete, the one closer to vulnerability wins. When two settlements compete, the one with the weaker external attention wins. When two interventions compete, the one closer to a household's daily survival wins.

HOW WE FIGURE OUT WHAT TO DO

A three-step common-sense loop. Nothing more.

No "theory of change". No logic models on the wall. Just this.

1.
Identify the need
Walk the settlement. Talk to households. Ask what is broken. Ask what they've already tried. Don't bring a solution in your bag.
2.
Assess our capacity to address it
Do we have a partner, a team member, a method, the money? Be honest. If the answer is no, say so. Don't pretend.
3.
Go after it
Plan it. Sequence it. Run it. Visit it. Course-correct it. Don't write papers about it. Don't wait for permission.

If a programme doesn't fit this loop — if it skips step 1, or pretends step 2, or stops at step 3 — it is not our work. It might be someone's work. It is not ours.

A SMALL WARNING

This sounds simple. It is not easy.

The hard part is staying inside the loop when everyone around you is doing something else.

Step 1 fails when
We walk in with a fundable idea and look for a community that fits it. We "discover" a need that happens to match what our donor is funding. We trust a survey done two years ago by someone else.
Step 2 fails when
We say yes because we want to look responsive. We assume a partner can do something they have never done. We forget that "capacity" includes money, time, supervision, and honesty about what will go wrong.
Step 3 fails when
We launch and never go back. We delegate visits to a junior and read a report. We let a quarterly review replace seeing the work with our own eyes.
WHAT WE ARE NOT TRYING TO DO

Equally important — the things we are not here for.

If you find yourself drifting into these, you are working for someone else.

Not preventive work
"Awareness", "behaviour change", "prevention before it happens" — almost always a way to look busy without committing to a visible outcome. We work where the harm is already happening and a household is already inside it.
Not advocacy work
Lobbying, position papers, policy submissions, conference statements. Important for someone — not us. The most vulnerable cannot wait for a policy cycle. We meet them where they live.
Not "larger-than-life" programmes
National rollouts. Pan-India ambitions. "Scaling models" before they have been done well in one place. We are suspicious of any plan that needs a slide on "replication strategy" before it has had its second cohort.
Not distant from the community
If the work can be done from a dashboard, an MIS report, or a quarterly review — it isn't our work. The closer the eye, the better the work. Distance is failure.
SO — IN ONE FRAME

A single page you can hold in your head.

If you forget everything else from today, remember this one.

We exist to support the most vulnerable communities. We do that by identifying a real need, honestly assessing our capacity to address it, and going after it on the ground.

We are not preventing, not advocating, not modelling, not scaling. We are doing.

QUESTIONS

Push on this.

15–20 minutes. The clearer you are now, the easier every later session is.

"What if a need is real but we can't address it?"
"What if a donor offers funding for advocacy or prevention — do we say no?"
"How do we know a need is real and not just the loudest voice in the settlement?"
"What about systems-level problems that won't get fixed on the ground?"
Or anything else. Don't save it for later.
